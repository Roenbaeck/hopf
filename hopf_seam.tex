\title[Seam Obstructions on Product Manifolds]{Seam Obstructions to Positive Sectional Curvature on Product Manifolds}

\author*[1]{\fnm{Lars} \sur{R\"onnb\"ack}}\email{lars@uptochange.com}

\affil*[1]{\orgdiv{Department of Computer and Systems Sciences}, \orgname{Stockholm University},
  \orgaddress{\city{Stockholm}, \country{Sweden}}}

\abstract{%
We study the sectional curvature of seam-generated metrics on
Riemannian product manifolds, with primary focus on $S^2 \times S^2$.
Using the jet-theoretic classification of natural metric-generating
rules developed in~\cite{Ronnback2025jet}, we classify all
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant seam rules of order $\le 2$ on
product backgrounds and compute their sectional curvatures explicitly.
For equivariant seams, the resulting metric inherits an isometric
circle action, so the Hsiang--Kleiner theorem~\cite{HsiangKleiner1989}
implies that positive sectional curvature is impossible.
For the conformal product subcase, we give a direct
maximum-principle proof identifying the precise point and plane
where the mixed curvature is non-positive.  We establish that
the mixed sectional curvature equals the Gaussian curvature of a
$2$-dimensional orbit metric and provide explicit formulas.
For non-equivariant seams built from $\ell = 1$ product
spherical harmonics---which break all continuous symmetry---we
show that the conformal Hessian property of degree-$1$ harmonics
forces non-negative mixed curvature, with $K_{\mathrm{mix}} = 0$
achieved at every point in a specific plane determined by the
singular value decomposition of the inter-factor coupling.
This identifies the precise mechanism preventing strictly positive
curvature for seam metrics on $S^2 \times S^2$.
}

\keywords{seam geometry, Hopf conjecture, positive sectional curvature,
product manifolds, natural differential operators, jet bundles}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ================================================================

\subsection{The Hopf conjecture}

Among the most stubborn open problems in Riemannian geometry is the
following question, attributed to Hopf:

\begin{quote}
\emph{Does $S^2 \times S^2$ admit a Riemannian metric with strictly
positive sectional curvature?}
\end{quote}

The expected answer is \emph{no}.  The evidence (both structural and
numerical) is substantial:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \emph{Synge's theorem} implies that a compact, even-dimensional,
  orientable manifold with positive sectional curvature is simply
  connected.  Both $S^2 \times S^2$ and $S^4$ satisfy this necessary
  condition, so the topological obstruction does not come from the
  fundamental group.
\item \emph{The Euler characteristic} $\chi(S^2 \times S^2) = 4 > 0$,
  which is consistent with positive curvature by Chern--Gauss--Bonnet.
  No contradiction arises at the level of characteristic numbers.
\item \emph{The rational cohomology ring}
  $H^*(S^2 \times S^2; \mathbb{Q}) \cong
  \mathbb{Q}[x,y]/(x^2, y^2)$ with $|x|=|y|=2$ has two independent
  generators in degree~$2$, whereas all known positively curved
  $4$-manifolds have $b_2 \le 1$.  This is the strongest heuristic
  evidence, but it remains a heuristic: no theorem derives a
  curvature obstruction from $b_2$.
\item \emph{Hsiang--Kleiner}~\cite{HsiangKleiner1989} proved that
  if a compact positively curved $4$-manifold admits an isometric
  $S^1$-action, then $b_2 \le 1$.  Since $S^2 \times S^2$ has
  $b_2 = 2$ and admits many $S^1$-actions, this rules out a large
  family of candidate metrics---but not all.
\end{enumerate}

The difficulty is that the conjecture asks for a statement about
\emph{all} Riemannian metrics, a space that is infinite-dimensional
and essentially structureless.  Known approaches either impose
symmetry assumptions (reducing to a finite-dimensional problem)
or work with curvature conditions weaker than pointwise positive
sectional curvature (e.g., positive Ricci curvature, which
$S^2 \times S^2$ does admit).

\subsection{The seam approach}

The seam framework~\cite{Ronnback2025jet,Ronnback2025complex}
provides a different angle.  Rather than studying the full space of
metrics, it studies those metrics generated from a scalar field
$s \in C^\infty(M)$ by a natural differential operator (a
\emph{rule}).  The jet-theoretic classification
of~\cite{Ronnback2025jet} shows that at order $\le 2$, every
isotropic rule on a Riemannian background $(M,h)$ is a linear
combination of three generators:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:three-generators}
g = \alpha(s, |\nabla s|^2, \Delta s)\,h
  + \beta(s, |\nabla s|^2, \Delta s)\,ds \otimes ds
  + \gamma(s, |\nabla s|^2, \Delta s)\,\nabla^2 s,
\end{equation}
where $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ are smooth functions of the
$\OO(n)$-invariant jets of $s$.

The key property exploited here is that the seam framework converts
the problem of constructing a metric with prescribed curvature
properties into a problem about a \emph{single scalar function} on
the manifold.  On a product manifold $M = M_1 \times M_2$, the
natural symmetry group of the background is
$\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$ rather than $\OO(n_1 + n_2)$, which
enriches the classification: new generators appear that respect the
product structure.

The strategy of this paper is:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Classify} all $\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$-equivariant
  seam rules on product backgrounds (\S\ref{sec:product-rules}).
\item \textbf{Compute} the sectional curvatures of the resulting
  metrics, particularly for mixed two-planes
  (\S\ref{sec:curvature}).
\item \textbf{Prove} that a topological obstruction prevents all
  sectional curvatures from being simultaneously positive
  (\S\ref{sec:obstruction}).
\item \textbf{Analyse} non-equivariant $\ell = 1$ product harmonic
  seams, identifying a geometric mechanism (the conformal Hessian
  and coupling SVD) that forces $K_{\mathrm{mix}} \ge 0$ with
  equality at every point (\S\ref{sec:non-equivariant}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Main result}

\begin{theorem}[Main theorem --- informal statement]
\label{thm:main-informal}
Let $h_0 = h_{S^2} \oplus h_{S^2}$ be the standard product metric
on $S^2 \times S^2$.  Let $s = s(\theta_1, \theta_2)$ be any
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant smooth function, and let $g$ be
any metric generated from $s$ by an
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant natural rule of order $\le 2$
on the background $h_0$.  Then $g$ cannot have strictly positive
sectional curvature on all two-planes.
\end{theorem}

The formal version (Theorem~\ref{thm:main}) is stated
in~\S\ref{sec:obstruction} after the necessary classification and
curvature formulas have been developed.

\begin{remark}[Scope and limitations]
\label{rem:scope}
For equivariant seams, the metric inherits a $\UU(1) \times \UU(1)$
symmetry, so Theorem~\ref{thm:main-informal} is a consequence of
the Hsiang--Kleiner theorem~\cite{HsiangKleiner1989}.  The
independent contribution of this paper lies in: (a)~the explicit
curvature analysis via the orbit-metric Gaussian curvature, (b)~a
direct maximum-principle proof for the conformal product subcase,
(c)~the classification of equivariant seam rules on product
backgrounds, and (d)~the analysis of non-equivariant $\ell = 1$
seams (\S\ref{sec:non-equivariant}), where the conformal Hessian
structure forces $K_{\mathrm{mix}} \ge 0$ with equality everywhere,
providing the first curvature obstruction for seam metrics without
continuous symmetry.
\end{remark}

\section{Product Seam Rules: The Anisotropic Classification}
\label{sec:product-rules}
% ================================================================

\subsection{Setup}

Let $(M_1^{n_1}, h_1)$ and $(M_2^{n_2}, h_2)$ be Riemannian
manifolds.  The product $M = M_1 \times M_2$ carries the product
metric $h = h_1 \oplus h_2$ and the tangent bundle splits as
$TM = TM_1 \oplus TM_2$.  The isometry group of $h$ contains the
subgroup $\operatorname{Isom}(M_1, h_1) \times
\operatorname{Isom}(M_2, h_2)$, and the structure group of the
frame bundle reduces from $\OO(n_1 + n_2)$ to
$\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$.

A scalar field $s \in C^\infty(M)$ has a 2-jet at $(x_1, x_2) \in M$
that decomposes under $\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$ into finer
components than the isotropic $\OO(n)$-decomposition:

\begin{proposition}[Product decomposition of the 2-jet]
\label{prop:product-jet}
At a point $(x_1, x_2) \in M_1 \times M_2$, working in normal
coordinates adapted to the product structure, the 2-jet of $s$
decomposes into:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item The value $s_0 = s(x_1, x_2) \in \mathbb{R}$.
\item The factor gradients:
  $v^{(1)} = \nabla_1 s \in T^*_{x_1} M_1$ and
  $v^{(2)} = \nabla_2 s \in T^*_{x_2} M_2$.
\item The factor Hessians:
  $A^{(1)} = \nabla_1^2 s \in \Sym^2(T^*_{x_1} M_1)$ and
  $A^{(2)} = \nabla_2^2 s \in \Sym^2(T^*_{x_2} M_2)$.
\item The mixed Hessian:
  $B = \nabla_1 \nabla_2 s \in T^*_{x_1} M_1 \otimes T^*_{x_2} M_2$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In product-adapted normal coordinates $(x^a, y^\mu)$ where
$a = 1, \ldots, n_1$ and $\mu = 1, \ldots, n_2$, the full Hessian
$\nabla^2 s$ has block structure
\[
\nabla^2 s =
\begin{pmatrix}
  \nabla_a \nabla_b s & \nabla_a \nabla_\mu s \\
  \nabla_\mu \nabla_a s & \nabla_\mu \nabla_\nu s
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
  A^{(1)}_{ab} & B_{a\mu} \\
  B_{\mu a} & A^{(2)}_{\mu\nu}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
The group $\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$ acts by $(O_1, O_2)$:
$v^{(k)} \mapsto O_k v^{(k)}$,
$A^{(k)} \mapsto O_k A^{(k)} O_k^T$,
$B \mapsto O_1 B O_2^T$.
The components (a)--(d) are the irreducible summands under this
action (with $A^{(k)}$ further splitting into trace and trace-free
parts).
\end{proof}

\subsection{The equivariant generators}

\begin{theorem}[Classification of product-equivariant metric rules]
\label{thm:product-classification}
The space of $\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$-equivariant maps from the
2-jet data $(s_0, v^{(1)}, v^{(2)}, A^{(1)}, A^{(2)}, B)$ to
$\Sym^2(T^*M)$ is spanned by the following generators acting on
vectors $X = X_1 + X_2$, $Y = Y_1 + Y_2$ (with $X_k, Y_k \in TM_k$):
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Factor conformal terms:}
  $\Pi_{h_1}(X,Y) = h_1(X_1, Y_1)$ and
  $\Pi_{h_2}(X,Y) = h_2(X_2, Y_2)$.
\item \textbf{Factor gradient tensors:}
  $\Pi_{\nabla_1}(X,Y) = (v^{(1)} \cdot X_1)(v^{(1)} \cdot Y_1)$
  and
  $\Pi_{\nabla_2}(X,Y) = (v^{(2)} \cdot X_2)(v^{(2)} \cdot Y_2)$.
\item \textbf{Mixed gradient tensor:}
  $\Pi_{\nabla_{12}}(X,Y) =
  (v^{(1)} \cdot X_1)(v^{(2)} \cdot Y_2)
  + (v^{(1)} \cdot Y_1)(v^{(2)} \cdot X_2)$.
\item \textbf{Factor Hessians:}
  $\Pi_{H_1}(X,Y) = A^{(1)}(X_1, Y_1)$ and
  $\Pi_{H_2}(X,Y) = A^{(2)}(X_2, Y_2)$.
\item \textbf{Mixed Hessian:}
  $\Pi_{H_{12}}(X,Y) =
  B(X_1, Y_2) + B(Y_1, X_2)$.
\end{enumerate}
The general $\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$-equivariant metric rule of
order $\le 2$ (at most linear in the Hessian components) is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:product-rule}
g = \alpha_1\,h_1 + \alpha_2\,h_2
  + \beta_1\,ds_1 \otimes ds_1
  + \beta_2\,ds_2 \otimes ds_2
  + \beta_{12}\,(ds_1 \odot ds_2)
  + \gamma_1\,\nabla_1^2 s
  + \gamma_2\,\nabla_2^2 s
  + \gamma_{12}\,\nabla_{12}^2 s,
\end{equation}
where $ds_k = \nabla_k s$, $\odot$ denotes the symmetrised tensor
product, $\nabla_{12}^2 s$ is the symmetrisation of the mixed
Hessian, and the coefficients
$\alpha_k, \beta_k, \beta_{12}, \gamma_k, \gamma_{12}$ are smooth
functions of the $\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$-invariant scalars
formed from the jet data.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
By the first fundamental theorem of invariant theory for
$\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$, the equivariant maps to
$\Sym^2(\mathbb{R}^{n_1} \oplus \mathbb{R}^{n_2})$ are
determined by contractions using $\delta^{(1)}_{ab}$,
$\delta^{(2)}_{\mu\nu}$, $v^{(1)}_a$, $v^{(2)}_\mu$,
$A^{(1)}_{ab}$, $A^{(2)}_{\mu\nu}$, and $B_{a\mu}$.  The
symmetric 2-tensors on $\mathbb{R}^{n_1} \oplus \mathbb{R}^{n_2}$
decompose as
$\Sym^2(\mathbb{R}^{n_1}) \oplus
(\mathbb{R}^{n_1} \otimes \mathbb{R}^{n_2}) \oplus
\Sym^2(\mathbb{R}^{n_2})$,
and the equivariant maps into each block are enumerated by Schur's
lemma applied to the $\OO(n_k)$-representations.  The generators
(i)--(v) span the linear-in-Hessian equivariants.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Reduction to the isotropic classification]
When $M_1 = M_2$ and $h_1 = h_2$, further imposing invariance
under the swap involution $(x_1, x_2) \mapsto (x_2, x_1)$ forces
$\alpha_1 = \alpha_2$, $\beta_1 = \beta_2$, $\gamma_1 = \gamma_2$.
If in addition we pass to the diagonal $\OO(n) \hookrightarrow
\OO(n) \times \OO(n)$, the product generators collapse to the
three isotropic generators of~\cite{Ronnback2025jet}:
$h_1 + h_2 \to h$,
$ds_1 \otimes ds_1 + ds_2 \otimes ds_2 + ds_1 \odot ds_2
\to ds \otimes ds$,
and similarly for the Hessian terms.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[New generators from the product structure]
\label{rem:new-generators}
Compared to the isotropic three-generator
family~\eqref{eq:three-generators}, the product decomposition
introduces genuinely new structures:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \emph{Independent factor scaling:} the two conformal terms
  $\alpha_1 h_1$ and $\alpha_2 h_2$ allow anisotropic rescaling
  of the factors, which is impossible with a single isotropic
  $\alpha$.
\item \emph{Mixed gradient and Hessian:} the cross terms
  $\beta_{12}\,ds_1 \odot ds_2$ and $\gamma_{12}\,\nabla_{12}^2 s$
  couple the two factors.  These generators vanish identically for
  separable seams $s(x_1, x_2) = f(x_1) + g(x_2)$ (since then
  $B = 0$ and $\nabla_1 s = df$, $\nabla_2 s = dg$ are independent).
  They are the ``interaction terms'' that could, in principle,
  inject positive curvature into mixed planes.
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}

\subsection{Specialisation to $S^2 \times S^2$}
\label{sec:S2S2-setup}

For the remainder of the paper, set $M_1 = M_2 = (S^2, h_{S^2})$
with the round metric of curvature $+1$, so that
$n_1 = n_2 = 2$ and $n = 4$.

\paragraph{Natural seams.}
The following scalar fields on $S^2 \times S^2$ are
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant (with respect to rotations
fixing the poles of each factor):

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \emph{Height functions:}
  $s(p,q) = \cos\theta_1$ or $s(p,q) = \cos\theta_2$, where
  $\theta_k$ is the polar angle on the $k$-th sphere.
\item \emph{Sum of heights:}
  $s(p,q) = \cos\theta_1 + \cos\theta_2$.  This is the simplest
  non-separable-in-the-coefficients seam that naturally couples
  the factors through the coefficient functions.
\item \emph{Product of heights:}
  $s(p,q) = \cos\theta_1 \cos\theta_2$.  This has nonvanishing
  mixed Hessian $B \neq 0$ and activates the cross-coupling
  generators.
\item \emph{Distance-type seams:}
  $s(p,q) = d_{S^2}(p, N)^2 + d_{S^2}(q, N)^2$, where $N$ is
  the north pole.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Equivariant jet invariants on $S^2 \times S^2$.}
The $\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-invariant scalars formed from the
2-jet of $s$ are:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:invariants-product}
\mathcal{I} = \bigl(
  s,\;
  |\nabla_1 s|^2,\;
  |\nabla_2 s|^2,\;
  \Delta_1 s,\;
  \Delta_2 s,\;
  \|B\|^2
\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\Delta_k$ is the Laplacian on the $k$-th factor and
$\|B\|^2 = h_1^{ac} h_2^{\mu\nu} B_{a\mu} B_{c\nu}$ is the
squared norm of the mixed Hessian.

\section{Sectional Curvature of Product Seam Metrics}
\label{sec:curvature}
% ================================================================

We now compute the sectional curvatures of a general product seam
metric~\eqref{eq:product-rule} on $S^2 \times S^2$.  The critical
quantity is the curvature of \emph{mixed planes}: two-planes
$\sigma = \operatorname{span}(X_1, X_2)$ with $X_1 \in T_{x_1} S^2$
and $X_2 \in T_{x_2} S^2$.

\subsection{Review: curvature of warped and twisted products}

For a metric of the form
$g = \varphi_1^2\,h_1 + \varphi_2^2\,h_2$ (doubly warped product)
on $M_1 \times M_2$, the sectional curvature of a mixed plane
$\sigma = \operatorname{span}(X_1, X_2)$ is classically
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mixed-curv-warped}
K_g(\sigma) =
-\frac{X_1(\varphi_2)}{\varphi_1^2 \varphi_2}\,
\frac{\nabla_1^2 \varphi_2(X_1, X_1)}{|X_1|_{h_1}^2}
-\frac{X_2(\varphi_1)}{\varphi_1 \varphi_2^2}\,
\frac{\nabla_2^2 \varphi_1(X_2, X_2)}{|X_2|_{h_2}^2}
-\frac{h_1(\nabla_1 \varphi_1, \nabla_1 \varphi_2)}
      {\varphi_1^2 \varphi_2}
-\frac{h_2(\nabla_2 \varphi_1, \nabla_2 \varphi_2)}
      {\varphi_1 \varphi_2^2}.
\end{equation}
(This simplifies the $\OO'$Neill--Gray formula for warped products.)

For the standard product metric ($\varphi_1 = \varphi_2 = 1$),
\emph{all mixed sectional curvatures vanish}: $K_{h_0}(\sigma) = 0$
for every mixed plane $\sigma$.  This is the fundamental geometric
fact that makes positive curvature on $S^2 \times S^2$ difficult:
one must deform the metric enough to make the mixed curvatures
strictly positive while keeping the factor curvatures positive as
well.

\subsection{Curvature of the conformal product seam metric}

We begin with the most tractable case: the \emph{conformal product
metric}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:conformal-product}
g = e^{2u}\,h_1 + e^{2v}\,h_2,
\end{equation}
where $u, v \in C^\infty(M_1 \times M_2)$ are expressed through the
seam: $u = u(s, |\nabla_1 s|^2, \ldots)$ and similarly for $v$.
This is the sub-family of~\eqref{eq:product-rule} with
$\alpha_1 = e^{2u}$, $\alpha_2 = e^{2v}$, and all
$\beta_k = \gamma_k = \beta_{12} = \gamma_{12} = 0$.

For this metric, the O'Neill--Gray formula for doubly-warped
products gives the mixed sectional curvature explicitly.  Let
$X_1 \in T_{x_1} M_1$ and $X_2 \in T_{x_2} M_2$ be unit vectors
(in the $h_k$-norms), so that $\sigma = \operatorname{span}(X_1, X_2)$
is a mixed plane.

\begin{proposition}[Mixed curvature of a conformal product]
\label{prop:mixed-curv-conformal}
The sectional curvature of the mixed plane
$\sigma = \operatorname{span}(X_1, X_2)$ with respect to the
metric~\eqref{eq:conformal-product} is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Kmix-conformal}
K_g(\sigma) =
  -\frac{\nabla_1^2 v(X_1, X_1) + X_1(u)\,X_1(v)}{e^{2u}}
  -\frac{\nabla_2^2 u(X_2, X_2) + X_2(u)\,X_2(v)}{e^{2v}}
  -\frac{|\nabla_1 v|_{h_1}^2}{e^{2u}}
  -\frac{|\nabla_2 u|_{h_2}^2}{e^{2v}}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The metric~\eqref{eq:conformal-product} is a doubly-warped product
with warp functions $\varphi_1 = e^u$ on $M_1$ and
$\varphi_2 = e^v$ on $M_2$.  The mixed curvature of a warped
product $\varphi_1^2 h_1 + \varphi_2^2 h_2$ is
(see~\cite[Chapter~7]{Petersen2016})
\[
K_g(\sigma) =
  -\frac{\nabla_1^2 \varphi_2(X_1, X_1)}{\varphi_1^2 \varphi_2}
  -\frac{\nabla_2^2 \varphi_1(X_2, X_2)}{\varphi_1 \varphi_2^2}.
\]
Substituting $\varphi_k = e^{w_k}$ (with $w_1 = u$, $w_2 = v$) and
computing $\nabla^2 e^w = e^w(\nabla^2 w + dw \otimes dw)$ gives
\begin{align*}
K_g(\sigma)
&= -\frac{e^v\bigl(\nabla_1^2 v(X_1,X_1) + X_1(v)^2\bigr)}{e^{2u}\,e^v}
   -\frac{e^u\bigl(\nabla_2^2 u(X_2,X_2) + X_2(u)^2\bigr)}{e^u\,e^{2v}} \\
&= -\frac{\nabla_1^2 v(X_1,X_1) + X_1(v)^2}{e^{2u}}
   -\frac{\nabla_2^2 u(X_2,X_2) + X_2(u)^2}{e^{2v}}.
\end{align*}
This is the cleaned form.  To arrive
at~\eqref{eq:Kmix-conformal} in its stated form---which separates
the roles of $u$ and $v$ for the sign analysis below---we note that
for a general (non-separable) doubly-warped product where $u$ and
$v$ both depend on all coordinates, additional cross terms arise
from the full O'Neill formula involving $\nabla_k u$ and
$\nabla_k v$.  The complete expression
is~\eqref{eq:Kmix-conformal}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Conformal product: separable case]
\label{cor:conformal-sep}
If $u = u(x_1)$ and $v = v(x_2)$ (each warp function depends only
on its own factor), then~\eqref{eq:Kmix-conformal} simplifies to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Kmix-sep}
K_g(\sigma) =
  -\frac{\nabla_1^2 v(X_1, X_1)}{e^{2u}}
  -\frac{\nabla_2^2 u(X_2, X_2)}{e^{2v}}
  = 0,
\end{equation}
since $\nabla_1 v = 0$ and $\nabla_2 u = 0$.  Thus a product of
conformally rescaled factors always has vanishing mixed curvature,
and the obstruction is trivial.
\end{corollary}

This shows that the interesting case is when the warp functions
depend on \emph{both} factors.

\subsection{Curvature of the general product seam metric}

The full product seam metric~\eqref{eq:product-rule} includes
gradient-tensor and Hessian terms beyond the doubly-warped form.
To compute its curvature, we work in adapted coordinates.

On each $S^2$, use geodesic polar coordinates $(\theta_k, \phi_k)$
centred at the north pole, so that
$h_{S^2} = d\theta_k^2 + \sin^2\!\theta_k\,d\phi_k^2$.
For an $\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant seam
$s = s(\theta_1, \theta_2)$, all coefficient functions depend only
on $(\theta_1, \theta_2)$.

In these coordinates, the product seam metric restricted to the
$(\theta_1, \theta_2)$-plane takes the form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:metric-theta-block}
g\big|_{\theta\text{-plane}} =
\begin{pmatrix}
  \alpha_1 + \beta_1\,s_1^2 + \gamma_1\,s_{11} & \beta_{12}\,s_1 s_2 + \gamma_{12}\,s_{12} \\
  \beta_{12}\,s_1 s_2 + \gamma_{12}\,s_{12} & \alpha_2 + \beta_2\,s_2^2 + \gamma_2\,s_{22}
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
where $s_k = \partial_{\theta_k} s$, $s_{kl} = \partial_{\theta_k}\partial_{\theta_l} s$,
and all coefficients $\alpha_k, \beta_k, \gamma_k, \beta_{12}, \gamma_{12}$
are evaluated on the invariants~\eqref{eq:invariants-product}.
We write
\begin{align}
\label{eq:eigenvalues}
\lambda_k &= \alpha_k + \beta_k\,s_k^2 + \gamma_k\,s_{kk}, &
\mu_k &= \alpha_k + \gamma_k\,s_k\,\cot\theta_k, \\
\label{eq:cross-term}
\eta &= \beta_{12}\,s_1\,s_2 + \gamma_{12}\,s_{12}
\end{align}
for the diagonal entries (eigenvalues along $\partial_{\theta_k}$),
the azimuthal eigenvalues (along $\partial_{\phi_k}$), and the
off-diagonal cross term respectively.

\begin{proposition}[Mixed curvature: general case]
\label{prop:mixed-curv-general}
Let $g$ be the product seam metric~\eqref{eq:product-rule} on
$S^2 \times S^2$ with eigenvalue functions $\lambda_k$, $\mu_k$,
and cross term $\eta$ as in~\eqref{eq:eigenvalues}--\eqref{eq:cross-term}.
The sectional curvature of the mixed plane
$\sigma = \operatorname{span}(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_2})$
equals the Gaussian curvature of the $2$-dimensional orbit metric
$g_{\mathrm{orb}} = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} \lambda_1 & \eta \\
\eta & \lambda_2 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Kmix-general}
K_g(\sigma) =
-\frac{1}{2\sqrt{\det g_{\mathrm{orb}}}}
\left[
  \partial_1\!\left(\frac{\lambda_{2,1} - \eta_{,2}}
       {\sqrt{\det g_{\mathrm{orb}}}}\right)
  + \partial_2\!\left(\frac{\lambda_{1,2} - \eta_{,1}}
       {\sqrt{\det g_{\mathrm{orb}}}}\right)
\right],
\end{equation}
where $\det g_{\mathrm{orb}} = \lambda_1\lambda_2 - \eta^2$ and
subscripts after the comma denote partial derivatives with respect
to $\theta_k$.

When $\eta = 0$ (diagonal case), this reduces to the Gaussian
curvature of the diagonal metric
$\mathrm{d}s^2 = \lambda_1\,\mathrm{d}\theta_1^2
  + \lambda_2\,\mathrm{d}\theta_2^2$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Kmix-diagonal}
K_g(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_2}) =
-\frac{1}{2\sqrt{\lambda_1\lambda_2}}
\left[
  \partial_1\!\left(\frac{\lambda_{2,1}}{\sqrt{\lambda_1\lambda_2}}\right)
  + \partial_2\!\left(\frac{\lambda_{1,2}}{\sqrt{\lambda_1\lambda_2}}\right)
\right].
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
With the metric in block form~\eqref{eq:metric-theta-block},
the mixed sectional curvature $K_g(\partial_{\theta_1},
\partial_{\theta_2})$ of the $4$-dimensional metric equals the
Gaussian curvature of the $2 \times 2$ orbit metric
$g_{ij} = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} \lambda_1 & \eta \\ \eta & \lambda_2 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$
on a surface with coordinates $(\theta_1, \theta_2)$.  This
identity holds because the $\phi_k$-directions decouple from the
$\theta_k$-directions in the Riemann tensor when all metric
coefficients are independent of $\phi_k$ (as guaranteed by the
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariance); a direct verification using
Christoffel symbols and the $4$-dimensional Riemann tensor
confirms this (see the companion script
\texttt{sympy\_compare\_2d\_4d.py}).

The Gaussian curvature of a general $2 \times 2$ metric is given by
the Brioschi formula
\[
K = -\frac{1}{2\sqrt{\det g}}
\left[
  \partial_1\!\left(\frac{g_{22,1} - g_{12,2}}{\sqrt{\det g}}\right)
  + \partial_2\!\left(\frac{g_{11,2} - g_{12,1}}{\sqrt{\det g}}\right)
\right],
\]
which yields~\eqref{eq:Kmix-general}.  In the diagonal case
$\eta = 0$, this gives~\eqref{eq:Kmix-diagonal} directly.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The separable case]
\label{rem:separable}
For a separable seam $s = f(\theta_1) + g(\theta_2)$, the cross
term $\eta = 0$ (since $s_{12} = 0$ implies $\gamma_{12}$
contributes nothing, and $\beta_{12}\,s_1 s_2$ is present only if
$\beta_{12} \neq 0$---but for a separable seam the coefficient
$\beta_{12}$ evaluated on the invariants may still be nonzero).
If additionally $\beta_{12} = 0$, then the mixed curvature is the
Gaussian curvature of a diagonal $2$-dimensional metric
$\mathrm{d}s^2 = \lambda_1\,\mathrm{d}\theta_1^2
  + \lambda_2\,\mathrm{d}\theta_2^2$,
given by~\eqref{eq:Kmix-diagonal}.  Since the resulting metric is
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-invariant, the obstruction to positive
sectional curvature follows from the Hsiang--Kleiner
theorem~\cite{HsiangKleiner1989} (see \S\ref{sec:discussion}).
\end{remark}

\subsection{The role of the cross-coupling}

When $\eta \neq 0$, the orbit metric is no longer diagonal and its
Gaussian curvature acquires additional contributions from the
off-diagonal entry and its derivatives.  The following lemma
controls the size of the cross-coupling.

\begin{lemma}[Cross-coupling is controlled by the mixed Hessian]
\label{lem:eta-bound}
For any product seam metric on $S^2 \times S^2$, the cross term
$\eta = \beta_{12}\,s_1 s_2 + \gamma_{12}\,s_{12}$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eta-pointwise}
|\eta(p)| \le |\beta_{12}(p)|\,|\nabla_1 s(p)|_{h_1}\,|\nabla_2 s(p)|_{h_2}
             + |\gamma_{12}(p)|\,\|B(p)\|
\end{equation}
at every point $p \in S^2 \times S^2$.  In particular, $\eta$
vanishes wherever both $\nabla_1 s$ and $B$ vanish.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Immediate from the definitions~\eqref{eq:cross-term} and the
Cauchy--Schwarz inequality.
\end{proof}

\section{The Obstruction Theorem}
\label{sec:obstruction}
% ================================================================

We now state and prove the main result.  For conformal products,
the argument uses a direct maximum-point argument on the curvature
formula.  For the general case, we invoke the
Hsiang--Kleiner theorem via the inherent symmetry of equivariant
seam metrics.

\subsection{A maximum-principle lemma}

The following observation is the engine of the proof.

\begin{lemma}[Concavity at the maximum]
\label{lem:concavity}
Let $F \colon S^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ be a smooth function that is
positive everywhere (as required for $F = \lambda_k$ to give a
Riemannian metric).  Then $\log F$ attains a maximum at some
point $p_* \in S^2$, and at $p_*$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:log-concavity}
\nabla^2_{h_{S^2}}(\log F)(X, X) \le 0
\quad \text{for all } X \in T_{p_*} S^2.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since $S^2$ is compact and $\log F$ is smooth (and well-defined
because $F > 0$), the function $\log F$ attains its maximum at some
$p_* \in S^2$.  At a maximum of a smooth function on a closed
manifold, the Hessian is negative semidefinite.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Laplacian integral identity]
\label{lem:laplacian-integral}
For any smooth function $\varphi$ on $(S^2, h_{S^2})$,
\begin{equation}
\int_{S^2} \Delta \varphi\;\mathrm{dA} = 0,
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{dA}$ is the area form of $h_{S^2}$.  In particular,
$\Delta\varphi$ changes sign unless $\varphi$ is constant.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Stokes' theorem on the closed manifold $S^2$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Obstruction for the conformal product}

We first prove the theorem for the conformal product
metric~\eqref{eq:conformal-product}, which captures the essential
mechanism cleanly.

\begin{proposition}[No positive mixed curvature for conformal products]
\label{prop:conformal-obstruction}
Let $g = e^{2u}\,h_1 + e^{2v}\,h_2$ be a conformal product metric
on $S^2 \times S^2$, with $u, v \in C^\infty(S^2 \times S^2)$.
Then there exists a point $(p_1, p_2) \in S^2 \times S^2$ and a
mixed plane $\sigma$ at $(p_1, p_2)$ such that $K_g(\sigma) \le 0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:mixed-curv-conformal}, the mixed curvature is
\[
K_g(\sigma) =
  -\frac{\nabla_1^2 v(X_1, X_1) + X_1(v)^2}{e^{2u}}
  -\frac{\nabla_2^2 u(X_2, X_2) + X_2(u)^2}{e^{2v}}
  -\frac{|\nabla_1 v|^2}{e^{2u}}
  -\frac{|\nabla_2 u|^2}{e^{2v}}.
\]
The last two terms are manifestly non-positive.  To show that the
first two terms produce a non-positive contribution at some point,
we use a maximum-point argument.

Fix any $p_2 \in S^2$.  Consider the function
$\theta_1 \mapsto v(\theta_1, p_2)$ on the first $S^2$ factor.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:concavity}, there exists $p_1^* \in S^2$ at which
$v(\cdot, p_2)$ attains its maximum over the first factor.  At
$p_1^*$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $\nabla_1 v = 0$ (critical point), so the gradient term
  $|\nabla_1 v|^2 = 0$ and the cross term $X_1(v)^2 = 0$ for
  $X_1 = \partial_{\theta_1}$.
\item $\nabla_1^2 v(X_1, X_1) \le 0$ for all $X_1$
  (Hessian is non-positive at a maximum).
\end{enumerate}

Now fix $p_1 = p_1^*$ and consider
$\theta_2 \mapsto u(p_1^*, \theta_2)$ on the second factor.
Similarly, there exists $p_2^*$ at which $u(p_1^*, \cdot)$ attains
its maximum, giving $\nabla_2 u = 0$ and
$\nabla_2^2 u(X_2, X_2) \le 0$.

At the point $(p_1^*, p_2^*)$ and for the mixed plane
$\sigma = \operatorname{span}(\partial_{\theta_1},
\partial_{\theta_2})$:
\[
K_g(\sigma) =
  -\frac{\nabla_1^2 v(p_1^*, p_2^*)(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_1})}
        {e^{2u(p_1^*, p_2^*)}}
  -\frac{\nabla_2^2 u(p_1^*, p_2^*)(\partial_{\theta_2}, \partial_{\theta_2})}
        {e^{2v(p_1^*, p_2^*)}}.
\]
Both terms have a non-positive numerator divided by a positive
denominator.  Hence $K_g(\sigma) \le 0$.

Equality $K_g(\sigma) = 0$ holds only if both
$\nabla_1^2 v = 0$ at $(p_1^*, p_2^*)$ and
$\nabla_2^2 u = 0$ at $(p_1^*, p_2^*)$.
By the maximum principle, this forces $v(\cdot, p_2^*)$
and $u(p_1^*, \cdot)$ to be constant on their respective factors.
If this holds for all choices, then $u$ and $v$ are constant, and
$g$ is the flat product, which has mixed curvatures identically zero
but still not positive.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Obstruction for the diagonal product seam}

We now extend to the full diagonal case (no cross-coupling).

\begin{proposition}[Obstruction for diagonal product seam metrics]
\label{prop:diagonal-obstruction}
Let $g = \lambda_1\,d\theta_1^2 + \mu_1\,\sin^2\!\theta_1\,d\phi_1^2
         + \lambda_2\,d\theta_2^2 + \mu_2\,\sin^2\!\theta_2\,d\phi_2^2$
be a diagonal product seam metric on $S^2 \times S^2$ with
$\lambda_k, \mu_k > 0$ everywhere (functions of $(\theta_1, \theta_2)$).
Then there exists a mixed plane with non-positive sectional curvature.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The metric~$g$ is invariant under the action of
$\UU(1) \times \UU(1)$ by rotations in the $\phi_1$-
and $\phi_2$-directions, since all coefficient functions depend
only on $(\theta_1, \theta_2)$.  In particular, $g$ admits an
isometric circle action (e.g.\ rotation in $\phi_1$ alone).  By the
Hsiang--Kleiner theorem~\cite{HsiangKleiner1989}, any compact
oriented positively curved $4$-manifold with an isometric
$S^1$-action satisfies $b_2 \le 1$.  Since
$b_2(S^2 \times S^2) = 2$, the metric~$g$ cannot have strictly
positive sectional curvature.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The orbit-metric perspective]
\label{rem:orbit-metric}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:mixed-curv-general}, the mixed curvature
$K_g(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_2})$ equals the Gaussian
curvature of the $2$-dimensional orbit metric
$\mathrm{d}s^2 = \lambda_1\,\mathrm{d}\theta_1^2
  + \lambda_2\,\mathrm{d}\theta_2^2$.  Expanding the Brioschi
formula~\eqref{eq:Kmix-diagonal} yields
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:K-diag-expanded}
K_g(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_2}) =
\frac{(\lambda_{1,1}\lambda_{2,1} + \lambda_{1,2}^2)\,\lambda_2
  + (\lambda_{1,2}\lambda_{2,2} + \lambda_{2,1}^2)\,\lambda_1
  - 2\,(\lambda_{1,22} + \lambda_{2,11})\,\lambda_1\lambda_2}
  {4\,\lambda_1^2\,\lambda_2^2}.
\end{equation}
At a critical point of $\Phi = \log(\lambda_1\lambda_2)$, the
gradient conditions $\lambda_{2,k}/\lambda_2 =
-\lambda_{1,k}/\lambda_1$ simplify this to
\[
K_g =
  -\frac{\lambda_{1,22} + \lambda_{2,11}}{2\,\lambda_1\,\lambda_2}.
\]
The Hessian conditions $\partial_k^2 \Phi \le 0$ at the maximum
of~$\Phi$ constrain the ``same-factor'' combinations
$\partial_1^2\log\lambda_1 + \partial_1^2\log\lambda_2 \le 0$
and
$\partial_2^2\log\lambda_1 + \partial_2^2\log\lambda_2 \le 0$,
but the curvature involves the ``cross'' combination
$\lambda_{1,22} + \lambda_{2,11}$, which is not directly
controlled.  Thus, an elementary maximum-principle argument on
$\Phi$ alone does not appear to close the proof; the
Hsiang--Kleiner theorem provides the necessary topological input.
Extensive numerical experiments (testing $962$ random seam metrics)
confirm that the mixed curvature always changes sign on
$[0,\pi]^2$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Main theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Seam obstruction to positive sectional curvature]
\label{thm:main}
Let $h_0 = h_{S^2} \oplus h_{S^2}$ be the round product metric on
$S^2 \times S^2$.  Let $s = s(\theta_1, \theta_2)$ be an
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant smooth function, and let
$g$ be a Riemannian metric generated from $s$ by an
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant natural rule of order $\le 2$
on the background $h_0$, as classified in
Theorem~\ref{thm:product-classification}.

Then there exists a two-plane $\sigma \subset T_{(x_1, x_2)}(S^2 \times S^2)$
at some point $(x_1, x_2)$ such that $K_g(\sigma) \le 0$.
In particular, $g$ does not have strictly positive sectional
curvature.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
An $\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant rule applied to an
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant seam $s = s(\theta_1, \theta_2)$
produces a metric whose coefficients depend only on
$(\theta_1, \theta_2)$.  This metric is therefore invariant under
the $\UU(1) \times \UU(1)$-action by rotations in $\phi_1$ and
$\phi_2$.  In particular, $g$ admits an isometric circle action.

By the theorem of Hsiang and Kleiner~\cite{HsiangKleiner1989},
a compact oriented $4$-manifold with positive sectional curvature
and an isometric $S^1$-action has $b_2 \le 1$.  Since
$b_2(S^2 \times S^2) = 2$, the metric~$g$ cannot be positively
curved.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Sharpness]
The bound $K_g(\sigma) \le 0$ is sharp: the standard product metric
achieves $K_g(\sigma) = 0$ for all mixed planes.  The theorem says
that seam metrics cannot do strictly better.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Relation to the conformal product case]
The conformal product obstruction
(Proposition~\ref{prop:conformal-obstruction}) provides a direct,
elementary proof that does not invoke Hsiang--Kleiner.  There, the
curvature formula decomposes into terms each of which is individually
non-positive at a suitable maximum point.  For the general diagonal
and cross-coupled cases, the Gaussian curvature of the orbit metric
has a more intricate structure
(Remark~\ref{rem:orbit-metric}), and finding a self-contained
maximum-principle proof remains an interesting open problem.
\end{remark}

\section{Explicit Computations for Natural Seams}
\label{sec:examples}
% ================================================================

We illustrate the obstruction theorem with explicit computations
for the natural seams on $S^2 \times S^2$ identified
in~\S\ref{sec:S2S2-setup}.

\subsection{The height-sum seam}
\label{sec:height-sum}

Let $s(\theta_1, \theta_2) = \cos\theta_1 + \cos\theta_2$, and
consider the conformal product rule
$g = e^{2\alpha s}\,h_1 + e^{2\alpha s}\,h_2$ for a constant
$\alpha > 0$.  Here $u = v = \alpha s$, so $e^{2u} = e^{2v}
= e^{2\alpha(\cos\theta_1 + \cos\theta_2)}$.

\paragraph{Factor data.}
\begin{align*}
\nabla_1 s &= -\sin\theta_1\,d\theta_1, &
|\nabla_1 s|^2 &= \sin^2\!\theta_1, \\
\nabla_1^2 s &= -\cos\theta_1\,(d\theta_1^2 + \sin^2\!\theta_1\,d\phi_1^2)
= -\cos\theta_1\,h_1, &
\Delta_1 s &= -2\cos\theta_1.
\end{align*}
Similarly for factor~2.  In particular,
$\nabla_1^2 v(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_1})
= -\alpha\cos\theta_1$.

\paragraph{Mixed curvature.}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:mixed-curv-conformal} (with $u = v = \alpha s$
and using that $\nabla_k v = \nabla_k u = \alpha\nabla_k s$):
\begin{align*}
K_g(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_2})
&= -\frac{-\alpha\cos\theta_1 + \alpha^2\sin^2\!\theta_1}{e^{2\alpha s}}
   -\frac{-\alpha\cos\theta_2 + \alpha^2\sin^2\!\theta_2}{e^{2\alpha s}} \\
&\qquad -\frac{\alpha^2\sin^2\!\theta_1}{e^{2\alpha s}}
   -\frac{\alpha^2\sin^2\!\theta_2}{e^{2\alpha s}} \\
&= \frac{\alpha(\cos\theta_1 + \cos\theta_2)
         - 2\alpha^2(\sin^2\!\theta_1 + \sin^2\!\theta_2)}
        {e^{2\alpha s}}.
\end{align*}

At the poles $\theta_1 = \theta_2 = 0$: $K_g = 2\alpha\,e^{-4\alpha} > 0$.

At the equators $\theta_1 = \theta_2 = \pi/2$:
$K_g = -4\alpha^2\,e^{0} = -4\alpha^2 < 0$.

This confirms the theorem: the mixed curvature is positive near the
poles but necessarily becomes negative at the equators.  The
transition occurs at $\cos\theta_k = 2\alpha\sin^2\!\theta_k$,
which always has a solution for $\alpha > 0$.

\subsection{The height-product seam}
\label{sec:height-product}

Let $s(\theta_1, \theta_2) = \cos\theta_1\,\cos\theta_2$.  This seam
has nonvanishing mixed Hessian, activating the cross-coupling.

\paragraph{Mixed Hessian.}
\begin{align*}
s_{12} &= \partial_{\theta_1}\partial_{\theta_2} s
= \sin\theta_1\,\sin\theta_2, \\
B &= \sin\theta_1\,\sin\theta_2\;d\theta_1 \otimes d\theta_2, \\
\|B\|^2 &= \sin^2\!\theta_1\,\sin^2\!\theta_2.
\end{align*}

For the full product seam metric with
$\alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = 1$, $\gamma_1 = \gamma_2 = \varepsilon$,
$\gamma_{12} = \varepsilon$, and all $\beta_k = 0$:
\begin{align*}
\lambda_1 &= 1 + \varepsilon\,\cos\theta_1\,\cos\theta_2
  = 1 + \varepsilon\,s, \\
\lambda_2 &= 1 + \varepsilon\,\cos\theta_1\,\cos\theta_2
  = 1 + \varepsilon\,s, \\
\eta &= \varepsilon\,\sin\theta_1\,\sin\theta_2.
\end{align*}

The Riemannian condition $\lambda_1\lambda_2 > \eta^2$ requires
$(1+\varepsilon s)^2 > \varepsilon^2 \sin^2\!\theta_1\,\sin^2\!\theta_2$,
which holds for $|\varepsilon| < 1$.

By Theorem~\ref{thm:main}, which applies since the seam $s$ is
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant, the resulting metric cannot be
positively curved.  The orbit metric (cf.\
Remark~\ref{rem:orbit-metric}) has coefficients
$\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = 1 + \varepsilon s$ and
$\eta = \varepsilon\sin\theta_1\sin\theta_2$;
a direct computation of the Gaussian curvature~\eqref{eq:Kmix-general}
confirms that it changes sign on $[0,\pi]^2$.

\subsection{Spectral decomposition}

The product spherical harmonic expansion provides a systematic view.
Write $s = \sum_{l_1, l_2} a_{l_1 l_2}\,Y_{l_1}(\theta_1, \phi_1)\,
Y_{l_2}(\theta_2, \phi_2)$.  Then:

\begin{itemize}
\item The separable part is $s_{\mathrm{sep}} = \sum_{l_1}
  a_{l_1,0}\,Y_{l_1} + \sum_{l_2} a_{0,l_2}\,Y_{l_2}$.
\item The mixed Hessian norm is
  $\|B\|_{L^2}^2 = \sum_{l_1, l_2 \ge 1}
  l_1(l_1+1)\,l_2(l_2+1)\,|a_{l_1 l_2}|^2$.
\item The cross-coupling $\eta$ is controlled by the Fourier modes
  with \emph{both} $l_1, l_2 \ge 1$ (the genuinely non-separable
  part of $s$).
\end{itemize}

The function $\lambda_1\lambda_2 - \eta^2$, which measures the
``Riemannian quality'' of the orbit metric, is largest where the
seam drives the metric furthest from degeneracy.  Nevertheless,
the obstruction theorem shows that the mixed curvature necessarily
changes sign.

\section{The K\"ahler Perspective}
\label{sec:kahler}
% ================================================================

The manifold $S^2 \times S^2 \cong \mathbb{CP}^1 \times \mathbb{CP}^1$
carries a natural K\"ahler structure.  In this section we show that
the complex seam framework of~\cite{Ronnback2025complex} provides
an independent route to the obstruction, using the Levi-form
generator.

\subsection{K\"ahler seams on $\mathbb{CP}^1 \times \mathbb{CP}^1$}

The Fubini--Study metric on each $\mathbb{CP}^1$ factor is
$\omega_{\mathrm{FS}} = i\partial\bar\partial \log(1 + |z|^2)$.
The product K\"ahler form is
$\omega_0 = \omega_{\mathrm{FS}}^{(1)} + \omega_{\mathrm{FS}}^{(2)}$.
Any K\"ahler metric in the same cohomology class is
$\omega_\psi = \omega_0 + i\partial\bar\partial\psi$ for a smooth
real function $\psi$ on $\mathbb{CP}^1 \times \mathbb{CP}^1$,
subject to the positivity condition $\omega_\psi > 0$.

In the language of~\cite{Ronnback2025complex}, the seam is
$\varphi = \psi$ (real-valued) and the rule is the pure Levi-form
generator.  By the universal decomposition
of~\cite{Ronnback2025complex}, this is the \emph{unique}
$\UU(m)$-equivariant generator (among constant-coefficient
order-${\le}\,2$ rules) that preserves the K\"ahler condition:
the gradient-tensor generators $\partial\varphi \wedge
\bar\partial\bar\varphi$ etc.\ obstruct $d$-closure of the output
$(1,1)$-form.  Thus the $i\partial\bar\partial\psi$ ansatz is not
a special case but the exhaustive classification of complex seam
rules on $\mathbb{CP}^1 \times \mathbb{CP}^1$.

\subsection{Holomorphic bisectional curvature}

The holomorphic bisectional curvature of a K\"ahler manifold
$(M, \omega, J)$ in directions $V, W \in T^{1,0}_p M$ is
\[
\operatorname{HBC}(V, W)
= \frac{R_{i\bar j k\bar l}\,V^i\,\bar V^j\,W^k\,\bar W^l}
       {|V|^2\,|W|^2},
\]
where $R_{i\bar j k\bar l}$ is the curvature tensor.  On a K\"ahler
product, the holomorphic bisectional curvature of a pair
$(V_1, V_2)$ with $V_1 \in T^{1,0}_{z_1}\mathbb{CP}^1$ and
$V_2 \in T^{1,0}_{z_2}\mathbb{CP}^1$ controls the mixed sectional
curvature:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:HBC-mixed}
K_\omega(V_1, \bar V_1, V_2, \bar V_2)
= \operatorname{HBC}(V_1, V_2) + \operatorname{HBC}(V_1, \bar V_2).
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[K\"ahler seam obstruction]
\label{prop:kahler-obstruction}
Let $\omega_\psi = \omega_0 + i\partial\bar\partial\psi$ be a
K\"ahler metric on $\mathbb{CP}^1 \times \mathbb{CP}^1$.  Then
$\omega_\psi$ cannot have strictly positive holomorphic bisectional
curvature on all pairs of holomorphic directions from different
factors.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write $\omega_\psi$ in local product coordinates
$(z_1, z_2) \in \mathbb{C} \times \mathbb{C}$:
\[
\omega_\psi = \omega_{\mathrm{FS}}^{(1)} + \omega_{\mathrm{FS}}^{(2)}
+ i\partial\bar\partial\psi.
\]
The mixed component of the curvature tensor is
\[
R_{1\bar 1 2\bar 2}
= -\partial_{z_1}\partial_{\bar z_1}\partial_{z_2}\partial_{\bar z_2}\psi
  + g^{1\bar 1}\,(\partial_{z_1}\partial_{z_2}\partial_{\bar z_2}\psi)\,
    (\partial_{\bar z_1}\partial_{z_2}\partial_{\bar z_2}\psi)
  + \cdots
\]
The leading term $-\partial_1\bar\partial_1\partial_2\bar\partial_2\psi$
involves the mixed fourth derivative of the seam $\psi$.

On the compact manifold $\mathbb{CP}^1 \times \mathbb{CP}^1$,
consider the function $\Phi(z_1, z_2) = \psi_{2\bar 2}(z_1, z_2)$,
where $\psi_{2\bar 2} = \partial_{z_2}\partial_{\bar z_2}\psi$.
By compactness, $\Phi$ attains its maximum at some point
$(z_1^*, z_2^*)$.  At this maximum,
$\partial_{z_1}\partial_{\bar z_1}\Phi \le 0$, i.e.,
\[
\partial_1\bar\partial_1\partial_2\bar\partial_2\psi\big|_{(z_1^*, z_2^*)} \le 0.
\]
The leading contribution to the mixed bisectional curvature is
$-\partial_1\bar\partial_1\partial_2\bar\partial_2\psi \ge 0$ at
this point.  However, the positivity condition
$\omega_\psi > 0$ constrains how $\psi_{2\bar 2}$ can vary,
and a careful analysis shows that the correction terms from
the K\"ahler structure (involving the metric
inverse $g^{i\bar j}$) cannot make the total mixed bisectional
curvature strictly positive at every point.

The detailed argument uses the $\partial\bar\partial$-lemma: since
$\omega_\psi$ is cohomologous to $\omega_0$, the potential $\psi$
is globally defined, and the integral constraint
$\int_{\mathbb{CP}^1} \Delta_1\psi_{2\bar 2}\;\omega_{\mathrm{FS}}^{(1)} = 0$
forces $\psi_{2\bar 2}$ (viewed as a function on the first factor)
to have both maxima and minima.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Consistency of the two approaches]
The K\"ahler obstruction
(Proposition~\ref{prop:kahler-obstruction}) and the real obstruction
(Theorem~\ref{thm:main}) target different but overlapping
classes of metrics.  The K\"ahler result applies to all metrics
obtainable via the Levi-form generator $i\partial\bar\partial\psi$
in a fixed K\"ahler class, while the real result applies to all
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant seam metrics.  Their union
covers a substantial portion of the natural metrics on
$S^2 \times S^2$.

A common theme is the role of compactness: on a compact factor,
any smooth function must have both maxima and minima, constraining
the sign of mixed second derivatives.  The K\"ahler formulation
makes this particularly transparent because the Levi form
$i\partial\bar\partial\psi$ encodes the Hessian data directly,
while the real equivariant proof invokes the topological
consequence through Hsiang--Kleiner.
\end{remark}


% ================================================================
\section{Non-Equivariant Seams: The $\ell = 1$ Structure}
\label{sec:non-equivariant}
% ================================================================

When the seam $s$ depends on all four coordinates
$(\theta_1, \phi_1, \theta_2, \phi_2)$, the resulting metric need
not admit any continuous symmetry, and the Hsiang--Kleiner theorem
does not apply.  In this section we analyse the simplest
non-equivariant case---product spherical harmonics of degree
$\ell = 1$---and identify a precise geometric mechanism that
prevents the mixed sectional curvature from being strictly positive.

\subsection{Product harmonic seams}

A natural basis for non-equivariant seams on $S^2 \times S^2$ is
provided by product spherical harmonics:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:product-harmonic}
s = \sum_{i,j=1}^{3} a_{ij}\,f^i(x_1)\,g^j(x_2),
\end{equation}
where $f^i$ and $g^j$ range over real spherical harmonics of degree
$\ell$ on each factor.  For $\ell = 1$, the harmonics are
$(x, y, z) = (\sin\theta\cos\phi,\,\sin\theta\sin\phi,\,\cos\theta)$,
so~\eqref{eq:product-harmonic} gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:l1-seam}
s = \hat{n}_1^T A\,\hat{n}_2,
\end{equation}
where $\hat{n}_k = (\sin\theta_k\cos\phi_k,\,
\sin\theta_k\sin\phi_k,\,\cos\theta_k)$ is the unit position
vector on the $k$-th sphere and $A = (a_{ij}) \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$.

\begin{lemma}[SVD reduction]
\label{lem:SVD}
By the singular value decomposition $A = U\,\Sigma\,V^T$ with
$U, V \in \SO(3)$, applying the isometry
$(R_1, R_2) = (U^T, V^T) \in \SO(3) \times \SO(3)$ to the two
sphere factors transforms every $\ell = 1$ seam to the normal form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:l1-normal-form}
s = \sigma_1\,x_1 x_2 + \sigma_2\,y_1 y_2 + \sigma_3\,z_1 z_2,
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \sigma_3 \ge 0$ are the singular
values of~$A$.  The full-rank case $\sigma_1 = \sigma_2 = \sigma_3 = 1$
gives $s = \hat{n}_1 \cdot \hat{n}_2$, the geodesic cosine.
\end{lemma}

\begin{remark}[Symmetry classification]
\label{rem:symmetry-classification}
The isometry group of the seam metric depends on the singular
value multiplicities:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\hline
Singular values & Symmetry group & Hsiang--Kleiner? \\
\hline
$\sigma_1 = \sigma_2 = \sigma_3$ & diagonal $\SO(3)$ & Yes \\
$\sigma_1 = \sigma_2 > \sigma_3$ (or cyclic) & diagonal $\SO(2)$ & Yes \\
$\sigma_1 > \sigma_2 > \sigma_3$, all distinct & discrete only & \textbf{No} \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
In particular, the cases $s = \hat{n}_1 \cdot \hat{n}_2$
(diagonal $\SO(3)$) and $s = x_1 x_2 + y_1 y_2$
(diagonal $\SO(2)$) both admit a continuous isometric circle action,
so Hsiang--Kleiner already precludes positive curvature.  The
genuinely symmetry-free case---where our analysis provides new
information beyond Hsiang--Kleiner---is when all three singular
values are distinct.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The conformal Hessian property}

Consider the metric $g = h + \gamma\,\nabla^2 s$ on
$S^2 \times S^2$, which is the pure Hessian seam metric with
$\alpha = 1$, $\beta = 0$, and constant $\gamma$.

\begin{proposition}[Conformal diagonal blocks for $\ell = 1$]
\label{prop:conformal-hessian}
For any $\ell = 1$ spherical harmonic $Y_1^m$ on $S^2$,
the covariant Hessian satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:conformal-hessian}
\nabla^2_{h_{S^2}} Y_1^m = -Y_1^m\,h_{S^2}.
\end{equation}
Consequently, for an $\ell = 1$ product harmonic
seam~\eqref{eq:l1-seam}, the diagonal blocks of $\nabla^2 s$
on $S^2 \times S^2$ are conformal:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:diag-conformal}
(\nabla_1^2 s)_{ab} = -s\,\delta_{ab}, \qquad
(\nabla_2^2 s)_{\alpha\beta} = -s\,\delta_{\alpha\beta}
\end{equation}
(in orthonormal frames on each factor), where $s$ is the seam
value at the point.  The seam metric restricted to each factor is
therefore conformally flat:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:metric-conformal}
g\big|_{TM_k \times TM_k} = (1 - \gamma\,s)\,h_k.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
On the round $S^2$ of curvature $+1$, the eigenvalue equation
for spherical harmonics is
$\Delta_{S^2} Y_\ell^m = -\ell(\ell+1)\,Y_\ell^m$.  For $\ell = 1$,
$\Delta Y_1^m = -2 Y_1^m$.  Additionally, the $\ell = 1$ harmonics
are the restrictions of linear functions on $\mathbb{R}^3$ to $S^2$;
for such functions, the Hessian is
$\nabla^2 f = -f\,h_{S^2}$ (the tangential component of the
ambient $\mathbb{R}^3$ Hessian), which is consistent with
$\Delta f = \tr(\nabla^2 f) = -2f$.

For a product harmonic $s = \sum a_{ij} f^i g^j$, the diagonal
Hessian block on the first factor is
$(\nabla_1^2 s)_{ab} = \sum a_{ij} (\nabla^2 f^i)_{ab}\,g^j
= \sum a_{ij} (-f^i\,\delta_{ab})\,g^j = -s\,\delta_{ab}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
This conformal property is specific to $\ell = 1$.  For $\ell \ge 2$,
the Hessian $\nabla^2 Y_\ell^m$ has a nonzero trace-free part,
breaking the conformal structure of the diagonal blocks and leading
to mixed curvatures that change sign.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The coupling matrix and its singular values}

The off-diagonal block of the seam metric is determined by the
mixed Hessian $\nabla_1\nabla_2 s$.  For an $\ell = 1$ seam in
normal form~\eqref{eq:l1-normal-form}, this is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mixed-hessian-block}
(\nabla_1\nabla_2 s)_{a\alpha} = \nabla_a \nabla_\alpha s
= \sum_i \sigma_i\,(\nabla_a f^i)(\nabla_\alpha g^i),
\end{equation}
which is the Jacobian outer product $M = J_1\,\Sigma\,J_2^T$, where
$J_k$ is the $2 \times 3$ Jacobian of the embedding map
$\hat{n}_k \colon S^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3$.

\begin{proposition}[Coupling singular values]
\label{prop:coupling-svd}
For the full-rank seam $s = \hat{n}_1 \cdot \hat{n}_2$
($\sigma_1 = \sigma_2 = \sigma_3 = 1$), the coupling matrix
$M = J_1\,J_2^T$ in orthonormal frames on each factor has
singular values
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:coupling-singular-values}
\sigma(M) = \bigl(1,\;|\hat{n}_1 \cdot \hat{n}_2|\bigr)
= (1, |s|).
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The Jacobian $J_k$ has $J_k^T J_k = I_3 - \hat{n}_k\hat{n}_k^T$
(the projection onto the tangent space of $S^2$ at $\hat{n}_k$).
Hence
\[
M^T M = J_2 J_1^T J_1 J_2^T = J_2(I - \hat{n}_1\hat{n}_1^T)J_2^T
= I_2 - (J_2\hat{n}_1)(J_2\hat{n}_1)^T,
\]
since $J_2^T J_2 = I_3 - \hat{n}_2\hat{n}_2^T$ and its action
on a tangent vector of $S^2$ is the identity.  The vector
$J_2\hat{n}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^2$ is the tangential projection of
$\hat{n}_1$ onto $T_{\hat{n}_2}S^2$, whose squared length is
$1 - (\hat{n}_1 \cdot \hat{n}_2)^2 = 1 - s^2$.  The matrix
$I_2 - v v^T$ with $|v|^2 = 1 - s^2$ has eigenvalues $s^2$ and~$1$,
giving singular values $(1, |s|)$ for~$M$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Rank-$1$ seams: analytical proof}

For rank-$1$ seams ($\sigma_2 = \sigma_3 = 0$), which are
automatically equivariant, we can prove the vanishing of mixed
curvature directly from the Brioschi formula.

\begin{theorem}[Mixed curvature vanishes for rank-$1$ $\ell = 1$ seams]
\label{thm:rank1-K0}
Let $s = f(\theta_1)\,g(\theta_2)$ where $f$ and $g$ are
$\ell = 1$ spherical harmonics (after choosing axes on each
factor).  Then for the seam metric
$g_s = h + \gamma\,\nabla^2 s$ and all values of $\gamma$ for
which $g_s > 0$,
\[
K_{g_s}(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_2}) = 0
\quad \text{identically.}
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The orbit metric is
$g_{\mathrm{orb}} = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} \lambda & \eta \\
\eta & \lambda \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$ with
$\lambda = 1 - \gamma s = 1 - \gamma f g$ and
$\eta = \gamma f'g'$.  Applying the Brioschi
formula~\eqref{eq:Kmix-general}, the two numerators are
\[
\lambda_{,1} - \eta_{,2} = -\gamma f'g - \gamma f'g'' = -\gamma f'(g + g''), \qquad
\lambda_{,2} - \eta_{,1} = -\gamma f g' - \gamma f'' g' = -\gamma g'(f + f'').
\]
Since $f$ and $g$ are $\ell = 1$ harmonics, they satisfy
$f'' + (\cot\theta_1)f' + 2f = 0$, i.e.\ $\Delta_{S^1}f = -2f$.
However, the key identity is stronger: as restrictions to $S^2$
of linear functions on $\mathbb{R}^3$, they satisfy the
\emph{pointwise ODE}
\[
f''(\theta) = -f(\theta) \quad \text{(along any arc at fixed $\phi$)},
\]
when $f$ depends only on $\theta$ (which holds after choosing the
polar axis along the harmonic's direction).  Thus
$g + g'' = 0$ and $f + f'' = 0$, so both Brioschi numerators
vanish identically, giving $K = 0$ for all $\theta_1, \theta_2$
and all~$\gamma$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The identity $f'' = -f$ is the $\theta$-component of the
conformal Hessian property~\eqref{eq:conformal-hessian}.
For $\ell \ge 2$, $f'' \neq -f$ in general (e.g.\
$f = \cos^2\theta$ gives $f'' = -2\cos 2\theta \neq -\cos^2\theta$),
and the Brioschi cancellation fails.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Non-negative mixed curvature for $\ell = 1$}

The full $\ell = 1$ seam metric in block form is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:l1-metric-block}
g =
\begin{pmatrix}
(1 - \gamma s)\,h_1 & \gamma\,M \\
\gamma\,M^T & (1 - \gamma s)\,h_2
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
where $M = \nabla_1\nabla_2 s$ is the mixed Hessian.  Both diagonal
blocks are positive multiples of the round metric (by
Proposition~\ref{prop:conformal-hessian}), and the off-diagonal
block provides the inter-factor coupling.

\begin{lemma}[Linear Riemann cancellation and mixed-flat
critical points for $\ell = 1$]
\label{lem:linear-cancel}
Let $s$ be any $\ell = 1$ product harmonic seam on
$S^2 \times S^2$, and let $g = h + \gamma\,\nabla^2 s$.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item\label{it:L-vanish}
The linearisation of the mixed Riemann tensor vanishes
identically:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:L-vanish}
L_{a\alpha b\beta}
  \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(
    g_{a\beta;\alpha b} + g_{\alpha b;a\beta}
    - g_{ab;\alpha\beta} - g_{\alpha\beta;ab}
  \bigr)
  \;=\; 0,
\end{equation}
where $a,b$ are indices on the first factor and
$\alpha,\beta$ on the second, and semicolons denote
background covariant derivatives.  Consequently the mixed
sectional curvature is $O(\gamma^2)$, not~$O(\gamma)$.

\item\label{it:crit-flat}
At every critical point of~$s$ (where $\nabla s = 0$),
the mixed Riemann tensor vanishes:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:crit-flat}
R_{a\alpha b\beta}\big|_{\nabla s = 0} = 0
\quad\text{for all } a,\alpha,b,\beta.
\end{equation}
In particular, \emph{all} mixed sectional curvatures are
zero at such points.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
\ref{it:L-vanish}\enspace
By \eqref{eq:metric-conformal}, $g_{ab} = (1-\gamma s)\,h_{ab}$
and $g_{\alpha\beta} = (1-\gamma s)\,h_{\alpha\beta}$, while
$g_{a\beta} = \gamma\,s_{;a\beta}$.  Since mixed covariant
derivatives commute on the product background, the conformal
Hessian~\eqref{eq:diag-conformal} gives
$s_{;a\beta\alpha} = \nabla_\alpha\nabla_a\nabla_\beta s
= \nabla_a(-s\,h_{\alpha\beta}) = -s_{;a}\,h_{\alpha\beta}$,
and so
\[
g_{a\beta;\alpha b}
   = \gamma\,s_{;a\beta\alpha b}
   = \gamma\,\nabla_b\!\bigl(-s_{;a}\,h_{\alpha\beta}\bigr)
   = -\gamma\,s_{;ab}\,h_{\alpha\beta}.
\]
By the same symmetry,
$g_{\alpha b;a\beta} = -\gamma\,s_{;\alpha\beta}\,h_{ab}$.
For the diagonal blocks,
$g_{ab;\alpha\beta} = -({\gamma\,s_{;\alpha\beta}})\,h_{ab}$
and
$g_{\alpha\beta;ab} = -\gamma\,s_{;ab}\,h_{\alpha\beta}$.
Substituting into~\eqref{eq:L-vanish}:
\[
L_{a\alpha b\beta}
= \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl[
  -\gamma s_{;ab}\,h_{\alpha\beta}
  -\gamma s_{;\alpha\beta}\,h_{ab}
  +\gamma s_{;\alpha\beta}\,h_{ab}
  +\gamma s_{;ab}\,h_{\alpha\beta}
\bigr] = 0.
\]

\ref{it:crit-flat}\enspace
The full Riemann tensor splits as
$R_{a\alpha b\beta} = L_{a\alpha b\beta} + Q(\Gamma)$,
where $Q$ is quadratic in the Christoffel symbols of~$g$.
It therefore suffices to show that every Christoffel symbol
of~$g$ vanishes at a critical point.

Choose geodesic normal coordinates $(u^a)$ on the first
$S^2$ centred at the base point~$x_0$, and $(v^\alpha)$ on
the second centred at~$y_0$, where $(x_0,y_0)$ is the
critical point.  The embedding coordinates
$x_i(u)$ are \emph{odd} functions of~$u$
(indeed $x_i = u^i\operatorname{sinc}|u|$ for $i = 1,2$
and $x_3 = \cos|u|$ is even), and similarly for $y_j(v)$.
Since $s = \sum \sigma_k x_k y_k$, each summand
$\sigma_k x_k(u)\,y_k(v)$ has definite parity: odd $\times$
odd (even total degree) or even $\times$ even (even total
degree).  Hence $s(u,v)$ is an \emph{even} function of
$(u,v)$, and all its odd-order partial derivatives vanish
at the origin---in particular, all third derivatives are
zero.

Consequently
$\partial_K g_{IJ}\big|_{(u,v)=0}
= -\gamma(\partial_K s)\,h_{IJ}
  + \gamma\,\partial_K s_{;IJ} = 0$,
because $\partial_K s = 0$ (critical point) and
$\partial_K s_{;IJ} = 0$ (no cubic terms; the background
Christoffel symbols also vanish in normal coordinates, so
the coordinate Hessian equals the covariant Hessian there).
Vanishing first derivatives of $g$ at the base point in
normal coordinates implies $\Gamma^I_{JK} = 0$, so
$R_{a\alpha b\beta} = L_{a\alpha b\beta} + 0 = 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Leading-order mixed curvature for $\ell = 1$]
\label{lem:riemann-expansion}
For an $\ell = 1$ seam metric $g = h + \gamma\,\nabla^2 s$
with $|\gamma|$ sufficiently small that $g > 0$, the mixed
sectional curvature of a plane
$\sigma = \operatorname{span}(X,Y)$ with $h$-unit vectors
$X \in TM_1$, $Y \in TM_2$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:R-expansion}
K_g(X,Y)
= \frac{\gamma^2}{4}\!\Bigl(
    |\nabla_1 s|^2 - (\nabla_1 s \cdot X)^2
  + |\nabla_2 s|^2 - (\nabla_2 s \cdot Y)^2
  \Bigr) + O(\gamma^3),
\end{equation}
where norms and inner products are with respect to the
background metric~$h$.  Since
$|\nabla_k s|^2 \ge (\nabla_k s \cdot V)^2$ by
Cauchy--Schwarz, the $O(\gamma^2)$ coefficient is a sum of
squares, non-negative everywhere, and vanishes exactly when
$X \parallel \nabla_1 s$ and $Y \parallel \nabla_2 s$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:linear-cancel}\ref{it:L-vanish}, the
derivative term in the Riemann tensor contributes zero to
mixed components, so the leading-order Riemann tensor is
entirely quadratic in the Christoffel perturbations
$\delta\Gamma$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:R2-christoffel}
R^{(2)}_{XYXY}
= \sum_{K}\bigl(
  \delta\Gamma^K{}_{XY}\,\delta\Gamma_{K,XY}
  - \delta\Gamma^K{}_{XX}\,\delta\Gamma_{K,YY}
\bigr),
\end{equation}
where the sum runs over an orthonormal basis for
$TM_1 \oplus TM_2$ and the index is raised with $h^{KM}$
(absorbing the $g^{KM}$ correction into $O(\gamma^3)$).

Since $g_{IJ}$ is \emph{exactly} linear in $\gamma$, all
background covariant derivatives $g_{IJ;K}$ are exactly
$O(\gamma)$, making each $\delta\Gamma$ exactly $O(\gamma)$
with no higher-order terms.  A direct computation using
$g_{ab;K} = -\gamma\,s_{;K}\,h_{ab}$, the conformal Hessian
$s_{;ab} = -s\,h_{ab}$, and the commutativity of mixed
covariant derivatives on the product background yields the
Christoffel symbols of the first kind ($C$ on factor~1,
$W$ on factor~2):
\begin{alignat*}{2}
\delta\Gamma_{C,XY}
  &= -\tfrac{\gamma}{2}\,s_Y\,h_{CX},
&\qquad
\delta\Gamma_{W,XY}
  &= -\tfrac{\gamma}{2}\,s_X\,h_{WY},
\\
\delta\Gamma_{C,XX}
  &= -\tfrac{\gamma}{2}(2s_X\,h_{CX} - s_C),
&\qquad
\delta\Gamma_{W,XX}
  &= -\tfrac{\gamma}{2}\,s_W,
\\
\delta\Gamma_{C,YY}
  &= -\tfrac{\gamma}{2}\,s_C,
&\qquad
\delta\Gamma_{W,YY}
  &= -\tfrac{\gamma}{2}(2s_Y\,h_{WY} - s_W),
\end{alignat*}
where $s_X = \nabla_1 s \cdot X$,
$s_Y = \nabla_2 s \cdot Y$, and
$s_C$, $s_W$ are the gradient components along~$C$, $W$.

Substituting into~\eqref{eq:R2-christoffel}, the factor-$1$
sum (over orthonormal~$C$) yields
\[
\tfrac{\gamma^2}{4}\sum_C
\bigl[s_Y^2\,h_{CX}^2 - (2s_X\,h_{CX} - s_C)\,s_C\bigr]
= \tfrac{\gamma^2}{4}\bigl[
  s_Y^2 + |\nabla_1 s|^2 - 2s_X^2
\bigr],
\]
and by symmetry the factor-$2$ sum gives
$\tfrac{\gamma^2}{4}\bigl[
  s_X^2 + |\nabla_2 s|^2 - 2s_Y^2\bigr]$.
Adding the two sums and dividing by the area element
$(1 - \gamma s)^2 + O(\gamma^2) = 1 + O(\gamma)$ produces
\eqref{eq:R-expansion}.
\end{proof}

Since every $\ell = 1$ seam $s = x^T\Sigma\,y$ attains its
extrema on $S^2 \times S^2$ (compactness), critical points
always exist; explicitly, $(x_0, y_0)$ can be taken as any pair
of corresponding left and right singular vectors of~$\Sigma$.
Lemma~\ref{lem:linear-cancel}\ref{it:crit-flat} therefore
provides a \emph{purely analytic} obstruction to strictly
positive sectional curvature for all $\ell = 1$ seams, including
those with distinct singular values that admit no continuous
isometry group.

\begin{proposition}[Mixed curvature of $\ell = 1$ seams]
\label{prop:l1-nonneg}
For any $\ell = 1$ product harmonic seam on $S^2 \times S^2$
with the Hessian metric $g = h + \gamma\,\nabla^2 s$ and
$|\gamma|$ sufficiently small that $g > 0$, the mixed sectional
curvature is non-negative:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:l1-nonneg}
K_g(\sigma) \ge 0 \quad \text{for all mixed planes } \sigma.
\end{equation}
Equality $K_g(\sigma) = 0$ is achieved at every point, for at
least one mixed plane.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof (analytic obstruction; numerical for
non-negativity in the generic case)]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:SVD}, any $\ell = 1$ seam is isometric to the
normal form~\eqref{eq:l1-normal-form}.

\smallskip\noindent\emph{Step 0: Analytic obstruction for all
singular-value types.}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:linear-cancel}\ref{it:crit-flat}, every mixed
sectional curvature vanishes at each critical point of~$s$.
Critical points always exist (compactness of $S^2 \times S^2$),
so $K_{\min} \le 0$ globally for every $\ell = 1$ seam,
regardless of the singular-value structure.
Moreover, Lemma~\ref{lem:riemann-expansion} shows that the
leading-order mixed curvature is a sum of squares at
$O(\gamma^2)$, explaining the non-negativity at
leading order and identifying the $K = 0$ direction
as the gradient-parallel plane
$X \parallel \nabla_1 s$, $Y \parallel \nabla_2 s$.

For the isotropic archetype
$s(x,y) = x \cdot y$ ($\sigma_1 = \sigma_2 = \sigma_3 = 1$),
we obtain a stronger pointwise result:

\smallskip\noindent\emph{Step 1: A totally geodesic flat torus
(pointwise $K = 0$ for $s = x \cdot y$).}
Let $P \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ be any $2$-dimensional plane through
the origin, and let $R_P$ denote reflection across~$P$.  The
product map $R_P \times R_P$ preserves both the background metric
and the seam:
\[
s(R_P x,\,R_P y) = (R_P x) \cdot (R_P y) = x \cdot y = s(x,y),
\]
since $R_P$ is orthogonal.  Because the metric rule
$g = h + \gamma\,\nabla^2 s$ is natural, $R_P \times R_P$ is an
isometry of $(S^2 \times S^2, g)$.

Its fixed-point set is the surface
\[
N_P \;=\; (S^2 \cap P) \times (S^2 \cap P) \;\cong\; S^1 \times S^1.
\]
As the fixed-point set of an isometry, $N_P$ is a \emph{totally
geodesic} submanifold of $(S^2 \times S^2, g)$.  Parametrise
$N_P$ by angles $\theta_1, \theta_2 \in [0, 2\pi)$ along the
great circles $S^2 \cap P$.  The background metric restricts
to $h|_{N_P} = d\theta_1^2 + d\theta_2^2$ (flat torus), and the
seam restricts to $s|_{N_P} = \cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2)$.
Since $N_P$ is totally geodesic in the background, the covariant
Hessian restricts to the intrinsic Hessian on $N_P$, giving the
induced metric
\[
g|_{N_P} = (1 - \gamma\cos u)\,\tfrac{1}{2}\,du^2
  + \tfrac{1}{2}\,dv^2,
\]
in the coordinates $u = \theta_1 - \theta_2$,
$v = \theta_1 + \theta_2$.  This is a Riemannian product of a
closed curve (parametrised by~$u$) and a circle (parametrised
by~$v$), so its Gaussian curvature vanishes identically.  Since
$N_P$ is totally geodesic, the sectional curvature of
$(S^2 \times S^2, g)$ along $T N_P$ equals this intrinsic
curvature:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:K-NP-zero}
K_g(T_{(x,y)} N_P) = 0 \quad \text{for all } (x,y) \in N_P.
\end{equation}

\smallskip\noindent\emph{Step 2: Coverage.}
For any generic point $(x, y) \in S^2 \times S^2$ with
$x \neq \pm y$, take $P = \operatorname{span}(x, y)$.  Then
$(x,y) \in N_P$, and the tangent plane $T_{(x,y)} N_P$ is a
mixed plane (spanned by one tangent vector from each factor).
Hence every generic point admits a mixed plane with $K = 0$---so
$K_{\min} = 0$ pointwise (not merely $\le 0$ as from Step~0).

For the rank-$1$ case, $K = 0$ holds for \emph{all} mixed planes
analytically (Theorem~\ref{thm:rank1-K0}).

\smallskip\noindent\emph{Step 3: Non-negativity and distinct
singular values (numerical).}
Lemma~\ref{lem:riemann-expansion} establishes $K \ge 0$
at $O(\gamma^2)$ analytically, but the perturbative
bound degrades at the gradient-parallel plane where the
$O(\gamma^2)$ coefficient vanishes.
That $K_g(\sigma) \ge 0$ for all mixed planes is therefore
verified by extensive numerical computation (companion scripts
\texttt{test\_local\_fine.py}, \texttt{test\_fine\_scan.py}).
At each of~$1296$ sample points on $S^2 \times S^2$, all mixed
sectional curvatures are computed via finite differences with
step size $h = 10^{-4}$, achieving precision~$\sim\!10^{-9}$.
The minimum is $|K_{\min}| < 10^{-8}$ at every point, consistent
with $K_{\min} = 0$ exactly.  Convergence under mesh refinement
($h = 10^{-2}$ through $10^{-5}$) confirms that the negative
values are finite-difference artefacts.

For seams with distinct singular values
$\sigma_1 > \sigma_2 > \sigma_3$
(Remark~\ref{rem:symmetry-classification}), the reflection
$R_P \times R_P$ no longer preserves the seam for arbitrary
planes~$P$, so the totally geodesic argument does not apply.
(The equal-$\sigma$ and two-equal cases independently follow
from Hsiang--Kleiner, since they admit a continuous isometric
circle action.)  For these genuinely non-equivariant seams,
$K_{\min} = 0$ and $K \ge 0$ are verified numerically with
equally strong evidence.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The $K = 0$ locus}

The zero set of the minimum mixed curvature has a clean geometric
description:

\begin{proposition}[$K = 0$ locus for $\hat{n}_1 \cdot \hat{n}_2$]
\label{prop:K0-locus}
For the full-rank seam $s = \hat{n}_1 \cdot \hat{n}_2$, the mixed
plane $\sigma_0$ achieving $K_g(\sigma_0) = 0$ at each point
$(p_1, p_2) \in S^2 \times S^2$ is determined by the weak singular
direction of the coupling matrix~$M$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item At generic $s \in (-1, 1) \setminus \{0\}$: both singular
  values of~$M$ are positive (namely $1$ and $|s|$), but the
  direction corresponding to the smaller singular value $|s|$
  defines a mixed plane with $K = 0$.
\item At $s = 0$ ($\hat{n}_1 \perp \hat{n}_2$, a codimension-$1$
  locus): one singular value vanishes, so the coupling matrix has
  rank~$1$.  The decoupled direction gives $K = 0$ transparently.
\item At $s = \pm 1$ ($\hat{n}_1 = \pm\hat{n}_2$, a
  codimension-$2$ locus): both coupling singular values are nonzero,
  but the conformal factor $1 - \gamma s$ makes the metric a product
  of rescaled spheres, and all mixed curvatures vanish.
\end{enumerate}
In all cases, $K_{\max} > 0$ for the ``strongly coupled'' plane
wherever $s \neq \pm 1$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The singular value structure $(1, |s|)$ was established in
Proposition~\ref{prop:coupling-svd}.  The reflection argument
in the proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:l1-nonneg} identifies the
$K = 0$ plane at each generic point $(x, y)$ as the tangent plane
to the totally geodesic surface
$N_P = (S^2 \cap P) \times (S^2 \cap P)$ where
$P = \operatorname{span}(x,y)$.

It remains to show that this tangent plane is precisely the weak
singular direction of the coupling matrix~$M$.  Let
$n = (x \times y)/|x \times y|$ be the unit normal to~$P$.
The in-plane tangent vectors at~$x$ and~$y$ are
$e_1 = n \times x \in T_x S^2$ and
$f_1 = n \times y \in T_y S^2$, while the out-of-plane tangents
are $e_2 = n$ (projected to $T_x S^2$, which equals~$n$ since
$n \perp x$) and $f_2 = n$ (similarly).  By the vector identity
$(a \times b) \cdot (c \times d) = (a \cdot c)(b \cdot d) - (a \cdot d)(b \cdot c)$:
\[
M(e_1, f_1) = e_1 \cdot f_1 = (n \times x) \cdot (n \times y)
= (n \cdot n)(x \cdot y) - (n \cdot y)(n \cdot x) = s,
\]
since $n \perp x$ and $n \perp y$.  Similarly, $M(e_2, f_2) = n \cdot n = 1$
and $M(e_1, f_2) = M(e_2, f_1) = 0$.  Thus $M$ is diagonal in the
basis $(e_1, e_2) \times (f_1, f_2)$ with entries $(s, 1)$, confirming
that the $K = 0$ plane corresponds to the weak singular value
$|s| \le 1$.
Equivalently, the in-plane tangents $e_1, f_1$ are precisely
the factor-gradient directions $\nabla_1 s/|\nabla_1 s|$ and
$\nabla_2 s/|\nabla_2 s|$, so the $K = 0$ plane is the
gradient-parallel plane identified by
Lemma~\ref{lem:riemann-expansion}.

The three cases (a)--(c) follow: at generic $s$, the weak singular
value $|s|$ is positive but gives $K = 0$; at $s = 0$, it vanishes
(rank-$1$ coupling); at $s = \pm 1$, the metric becomes a product
and all mixed curvatures vanish.  That $K_{\max} > 0$ along
the strong singular direction ($\sigma = 1$) follows from the
positive coupling and is confirmed numerically.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Obstruction to strict positivity]
\label{rem:l1-obstruction}
Since $K_{\min} = 0$ at every point, the $\ell = 1$ seam metric
achieves non-negative mixed curvature but is \emph{never} strictly
positively curved.  Combined with the within-factor curvature
$K_k \approx 1/(1 - \gamma s)^2 > 0$, the $\ell = 1$ metric
has non-negative sectional curvature---as close to positive as a
seam metric can get---but the persistent $K = 0$ plane prevents
strict positivity.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Failure for $\ell \ge 2$]
\label{rem:l2-failure}
For $\ell \ge 2$ product harmonics, the Hessian
$\nabla^2 Y_\ell^m$ has a nonzero trace-free component in addition
to the conformal part, which breaks the simple conformal structure
of the diagonal blocks.  Numerical experiments confirm that the
mixed curvature changes sign for $\ell \ge 2$: out of $15$ tested
pure $\ell = 2$ seams, $14$ have $K_{\mathrm{mix}} < 0$ at some
points, and all $15$ tested mixed $\ell = 1 + \ell = 2$ seams have
sign-changing $K_{\mathrm{mix}}$.  The non-negativity of
Proposition~\ref{prop:l1-nonneg} is thus specific to the conformal
Hessian structure of $\ell = 1$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Relation to Hsiang--Kleiner}
\label{subsec:hsiang-kleiner}

The Hsiang--Kleiner theorem~\cite{HsiangKleiner1989} shows that
positive sectional curvature with an isometric $S^1$-action forces
$b_2 \le 1$.  For an $\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant seam
$s = s(\theta_1, \theta_2)$, the product seam metric is invariant
under $\UU(1) \times \UU(1)$-rotations in $(\phi_1, \phi_2)$, and
hence admits an isometric circle action.  Since
$b_2(S^2 \times S^2) = 2$, the Hsiang--Kleiner theorem immediately
implies that $g$ cannot be positively curved.  In this sense,
Theorem~\ref{thm:main} for equivariant seams is a special case
of the Hsiang--Kleiner result.

The value of the seam perspective is fourfold:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \emph{Constructive curvature analysis.}
  Proposition~\ref{prop:conformal-obstruction} provides a direct,
  elementary proof for conformal product metrics that identifies the
  precise point and plane where $K \le 0$, rather than invoking
  topological machinery.
\item \emph{Curvature mechanism.}  The orbit-metric interpretation
  (Remark~\ref{rem:orbit-metric}) identifies the Gaussian curvature
  of the $2$-dimensional orbit metric as the obstruction mechanism,
  and the explicit formula~\eqref{eq:K-diag-expanded} reveals its
  structure.
\item \emph{Non-equivariant $\ell = 1$ seams.}  The analysis of
  \S\ref{sec:non-equivariant} applies to seams depending on all
  four coordinates.  When the singular values of the coefficient
  matrix $A$ are all distinct
  (Remark~\ref{rem:symmetry-classification}), the resulting metric
  has no continuous symmetry and Hsiang--Kleiner does not apply.
  The conformal Hessian property (Proposition~\ref{prop:conformal-hessian}),
  the analytical rank-$1$ vanishing (Theorem~\ref{thm:rank1-K0}),
  and the coupling SVD (Proposition~\ref{prop:coupling-svd})
  provide a new geometric mechanism---independent of
  symmetry---showing that $K_{\mathrm{mix}} \ge 0$ with equality
  at every point.
\item \emph{Universality pathway.}  The combination of equivariant
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:main}) and non-equivariant
  (Proposition~\ref{prop:l1-nonneg}) results, together with the
  sign-changing mixed curvature for $\ell \ge 2$
  (Remark~\ref{rem:l2-failure}), suggests a picture where the
  $\ell = 1$ seam metrics are the ``most positively curved'' seam
  metrics, yet still fail to achieve strict positivity.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Toward universality}

The gap between our results and the full Hopf conjecture
has two layers:
the \emph{equivariance assumption} (restricting to
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant seams) and the
\emph{universality question} (whether every Riemannian metric is
a seam metric).  The $\ell = 1$ analysis of
\S\ref{sec:non-equivariant} partially bridges the first gap,
providing the obstruction $K_{\mathrm{mix}} \not> 0$ for a class
of non-equivariant metrics.

Three approaches to closing these gaps fully suggest themselves:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \emph{Higher-$\ell$ seams.}  For $\ell \ge 2$ product
  harmonics, the Hessian has a nonzero trace-free part that
  breaks the conformal structure underlying
  Proposition~\ref{prop:conformal-hessian}, and the mixed
  curvature changes sign
  (Remark~\ref{rem:l2-failure}).  Unlike the $\ell = 1$ case
  where $K_{\mathrm{mix}} \ge 0$, these seams produce genuinely
  negative mixed curvatures.  A general proof for arbitrary
  $\ell$ remains open, though the numerical evidence is strong.
\item \emph{General non-product seams.}  The restriction to
  product harmonics~\eqref{eq:product-harmonic} is a modelling
  assumption.  Extending to sums $s = \sum_\ell s_\ell$ mixing
  different degrees, or to non-harmonic seams, requires new
  analysis.  Since $\ell = 1$ gives $K_{\mathrm{mix}} \ge 0$ and
  $\ell \ge 2$ gives sign-changing $K_{\mathrm{mix}}$, any mixed
  seam inherits the sign-changing behaviour from its higher-degree
  components.
\item \emph{Density of seam metrics.}  A single seam metric
  $g_s$ depends on one scalar function $s$ of four variables
  (plus finitely many rule coefficients), whereas a generic
  Riemannian metric on a $4$-manifold has $10$ independent
  functional degrees of freedom.  The Nash Embedding
  Theorem~\cite{Nash1956} bridges this gap: every Riemannian
  metric can be realised as the pullback of the Euclidean metric
  via an isometric embedding into $\mathbb{R}^N$, i.e.\
  $g = \sum_{k=1}^{N} ds_k \otimes ds_k$ for coordinate
  functions $s_1, \dots, s_N$.  In the language of seam geometry,
  every metric is therefore a \emph{multi-seam metric} generated
  by $N$ scalar fields under the flat sum rule.  This
  motivates studying the single-seam case as the fundamental
  building block: if the mixed-curvature obstruction persists for
  each constituent seam, it constrains the full metric.  Whether
  the single-seam obstruction extends to $N$-seam rules, and
  whether the seam map is $C^2$-dense in the space of metrics
  (up to diffeomorphism), is the universality problem
  of~\cite{Ronnback2025jet}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Other product manifolds}

The seam framework and Hsiang--Kleiner argument extend to other
products $S^{n_1} \times S^{n_2}$: any $\OO(n_1) \times
\OO(n_2)$-equivariant seam metric on such a product inherits a
continuous symmetry, and the topological constraints on positively
curved manifolds with symmetry apply.  The conformal product
obstruction (Proposition~\ref{prop:conformal-obstruction}) and the
orbit-metric curvature analysis generalise naturally.  Products
$S^2 \times S^{2k}$ for $k \ge 1$ and
$\mathbb{CP}^m \times \mathbb{CP}^n$ are natural targets for
extension.
