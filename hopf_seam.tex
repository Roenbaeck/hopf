\title[Seam Obstructions on Product Manifolds]{Seam Obstructions to Positive Sectional Curvature on Product Manifolds}

\author*[1]{\fnm{Lars} \sur{R\"onnb\"ack}}\email{lars@uptochange.com}

\affil*[1]{\orgdiv{Department of Computer and Systems Sciences}, \orgname{Stockholm University},
  \orgaddress{\city{Stockholm}, \country{Sweden}}}

\abstract{%
We study the sectional curvature of seam-generated metrics on
Riemannian product manifolds, with primary focus on $S^2 \times S^2$.
Using the jet-theoretic classification of natural metric-generating
rules developed in~\cite{Ronnback2025jet}, we classify all
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant seam rules of order $\le 2$ on
product backgrounds and compute their sectional curvatures explicitly.
We prove that no seam metric in this class achieves positive
sectional curvature on all two-planes: the mixed planes spanning
both factors necessarily contain a non-positive direction.
The obstruction is topological in character, arising from a
Gauss--Bonnet integral constraint on the seam coefficients,
and provides a partial result toward the Hopf conjecture that
$S^2 \times S^2$ admits no metric of strictly positive sectional
curvature.
}

\keywords{seam geometry, Hopf conjecture, positive sectional curvature,
product manifolds, natural differential operators, jet bundles}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ================================================================

\subsection{The Hopf conjecture}

Among the most stubborn open problems in Riemannian geometry is the
following question, attributed to Hopf:

\begin{quote}
\emph{Does $S^2 \times S^2$ admit a Riemannian metric with strictly
positive sectional curvature?}
\end{quote}

The expected answer is \emph{no}.  The evidence (both structural and
numerical) is substantial:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \emph{Synge's theorem} implies that a compact, even-dimensional,
  orientable manifold with positive sectional curvature is simply
  connected.  Both $S^2 \times S^2$ and $S^4$ satisfy this necessary
  condition, so the topological obstruction does not come from the
  fundamental group.
\item \emph{The Euler characteristic} $\chi(S^2 \times S^2) = 4 > 0$,
  which is consistent with positive curvature by Chern--Gauss--Bonnet.
  No contradiction arises at the level of characteristic numbers.
\item \emph{The rational cohomology ring}
  $H^*(S^2 \times S^2; \mathbb{Q}) \cong
  \mathbb{Q}[x,y]/(x^2, y^2)$ with $|x|=|y|=2$ has two independent
  generators in degree~$2$, whereas all known positively curved
  $4$-manifolds have $b_2 \le 1$.  This is the strongest heuristic
  evidence, but it remains a heuristic: no theorem derives a
  curvature obstruction from $b_2$.
\item \emph{Hsiang--Kleiner}~\cite{HsiangKleiner1989} proved that
  if a compact positively curved $4$-manifold admits an isometric
  $S^1$-action, then $b_2 \le 1$.  Since $S^2 \times S^2$ has
  $b_2 = 2$ and admits many $S^1$-actions, this rules out a large
  family of candidate metrics---but not all.
\end{enumerate}

The difficulty is that the conjecture asks for a statement about
\emph{all} Riemannian metrics, a space that is infinite-dimensional
and essentially structureless.  Known approaches either impose
symmetry assumptions (reducing to a finite-dimensional problem)
or work with curvature conditions weaker than pointwise positive
sectional curvature (e.g., positive Ricci curvature, which
$S^2 \times S^2$ does admit).

\subsection{The seam approach}

The seam framework~\cite{Ronnback2025jet,Ronnback2025complex}
provides a different angle.  Rather than studying the full space of
metrics, it studies those metrics generated from a scalar field
$s \in C^\infty(M)$ by a natural differential operator (a
\emph{rule}).  The jet-theoretic classification
of~\cite{Ronnback2025jet} shows that at order $\le 2$, every
isotropic rule on a Riemannian background $(M,h)$ is a linear
combination of three generators:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:three-generators}
g = \alpha(s, |\nabla s|^2, \Delta s)\,h
  + \beta(s, |\nabla s|^2, \Delta s)\,ds \otimes ds
  + \gamma(s, |\nabla s|^2, \Delta s)\,\nabla^2 s,
\end{equation}
where $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ are smooth functions of the
$\OO(n)$-invariant jets of $s$.

The key property exploited here is that the seam framework converts
the problem of constructing a metric with prescribed curvature
properties into a problem about a \emph{single scalar function} on
the manifold.  On a product manifold $M = M_1 \times M_2$, the
natural symmetry group of the background is
$\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$ rather than $\OO(n_1 + n_2)$, which
enriches the classification: new generators appear that respect the
product structure.

The strategy of this paper is:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Classify} all $\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$-equivariant
  seam rules on product backgrounds (\S\ref{sec:product-rules}).
\item \textbf{Compute} the sectional curvatures of the resulting
  metrics, particularly for mixed two-planes
  (\S\ref{sec:curvature}).
\item \textbf{Prove} that a topological obstruction prevents all
  sectional curvatures from being simultaneously positive
  (\S\ref{sec:obstruction}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Main result}

\begin{theorem}[Main theorem --- informal statement]
\label{thm:main-informal}
Let $h_0 = h_{S^2} \oplus h_{S^2}$ be the standard product metric
on $S^2 \times S^2$.  Let $s \in C^\infty(S^2 \times S^2)$ be any
smooth function, and let $g$ be any metric generated from $s$ by
an $\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant natural rule of order $\le 2$
on the background $h_0$.  Then $g$ cannot have strictly positive
sectional curvature on all two-planes.
\end{theorem}

The formal version (Theorem~\ref{thm:main}) is stated
in~\S\ref{sec:obstruction} after the necessary classification and
curvature formulas have been developed.

\begin{remark}[Scope and limitations]
Theorem~\ref{thm:main-informal} is a \emph{partial result} toward
the Hopf conjecture: it rules out positive sectional curvature for
the class of seam-generated metrics, not for all Riemannian metrics.
The gap is the universality question: whether every Riemannian
metric on $S^2 \times S^2$ is (up to diffeomorphism) a seam metric.
This is an open problem even for the three-generator isotropic
family on general compact $4$-manifolds
(see~\cite[Open Problem~1]{Ronnback2025jet}).  However, the
obstruction mechanism we identify is topological (it uses the Euler
class and Gauss--Bonnet) and may extend beyond the seam class.
\end{remark}

\section{Product Seam Rules: The Anisotropic Classification}
\label{sec:product-rules}
% ================================================================

\subsection{Setup}

Let $(M_1^{n_1}, h_1)$ and $(M_2^{n_2}, h_2)$ be Riemannian
manifolds.  The product $M = M_1 \times M_2$ carries the product
metric $h = h_1 \oplus h_2$ and the tangent bundle splits as
$TM = TM_1 \oplus TM_2$.  The isometry group of $h$ contains the
subgroup $\operatorname{Isom}(M_1, h_1) \times
\operatorname{Isom}(M_2, h_2)$, and the structure group of the
frame bundle reduces from $\OO(n_1 + n_2)$ to
$\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$.

A scalar field $s \in C^\infty(M)$ has a 2-jet at $(x_1, x_2) \in M$
that decomposes under $\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$ into finer
components than the isotropic $\OO(n)$-decomposition:

\begin{proposition}[Product decomposition of the 2-jet]
\label{prop:product-jet}
At a point $(x_1, x_2) \in M_1 \times M_2$, working in normal
coordinates adapted to the product structure, the 2-jet of $s$
decomposes into:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item The value $s_0 = s(x_1, x_2) \in \mathbb{R}$.
\item The factor gradients:
  $v^{(1)} = \nabla_1 s \in T^*_{x_1} M_1$ and
  $v^{(2)} = \nabla_2 s \in T^*_{x_2} M_2$.
\item The factor Hessians:
  $A^{(1)} = \nabla_1^2 s \in \Sym^2(T^*_{x_1} M_1)$ and
  $A^{(2)} = \nabla_2^2 s \in \Sym^2(T^*_{x_2} M_2)$.
\item The mixed Hessian:
  $B = \nabla_1 \nabla_2 s \in T^*_{x_1} M_1 \otimes T^*_{x_2} M_2$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In product-adapted normal coordinates $(x^a, y^\mu)$ where
$a = 1, \ldots, n_1$ and $\mu = 1, \ldots, n_2$, the full Hessian
$\nabla^2 s$ has block structure
\[
\nabla^2 s =
\begin{pmatrix}
  \nabla_a \nabla_b s & \nabla_a \nabla_\mu s \\
  \nabla_\mu \nabla_a s & \nabla_\mu \nabla_\nu s
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
  A^{(1)}_{ab} & B_{a\mu} \\
  B_{\mu a} & A^{(2)}_{\mu\nu}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
The group $\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$ acts by $(O_1, O_2)$:
$v^{(k)} \mapsto O_k v^{(k)}$,
$A^{(k)} \mapsto O_k A^{(k)} O_k^T$,
$B \mapsto O_1 B O_2^T$.
The components (a)--(d) are the irreducible summands under this
action (with $A^{(k)}$ further splitting into trace and trace-free
parts).
\end{proof}

\subsection{The equivariant generators}

\begin{theorem}[Classification of product-equivariant metric rules]
\label{thm:product-classification}
The space of $\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$-equivariant maps from the
2-jet data $(s_0, v^{(1)}, v^{(2)}, A^{(1)}, A^{(2)}, B)$ to
$\Sym^2(T^*M)$ is spanned by the following generators acting on
vectors $X = X_1 + X_2$, $Y = Y_1 + Y_2$ (with $X_k, Y_k \in TM_k$):
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \textbf{Factor conformal terms:}
  $\Pi_{h_1}(X,Y) = h_1(X_1, Y_1)$ and
  $\Pi_{h_2}(X,Y) = h_2(X_2, Y_2)$.
\item \textbf{Factor gradient tensors:}
  $\Pi_{\nabla_1}(X,Y) = (v^{(1)} \cdot X_1)(v^{(1)} \cdot Y_1)$
  and
  $\Pi_{\nabla_2}(X,Y) = (v^{(2)} \cdot X_2)(v^{(2)} \cdot Y_2)$.
\item \textbf{Mixed gradient tensor:}
  $\Pi_{\nabla_{12}}(X,Y) =
  (v^{(1)} \cdot X_1)(v^{(2)} \cdot Y_2)
  + (v^{(1)} \cdot Y_1)(v^{(2)} \cdot X_2)$.
\item \textbf{Factor Hessians:}
  $\Pi_{H_1}(X,Y) = A^{(1)}(X_1, Y_1)$ and
  $\Pi_{H_2}(X,Y) = A^{(2)}(X_2, Y_2)$.
\item \textbf{Mixed Hessian:}
  $\Pi_{H_{12}}(X,Y) =
  B(X_1, Y_2) + B(Y_1, X_2)$.
\end{enumerate}
The general $\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$-equivariant metric rule of
order $\le 2$ (at most linear in the Hessian components) is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:product-rule}
g = \alpha_1\,h_1 + \alpha_2\,h_2
  + \beta_1\,ds_1 \otimes ds_1
  + \beta_2\,ds_2 \otimes ds_2
  + \beta_{12}\,(ds_1 \odot ds_2)
  + \gamma_1\,\nabla_1^2 s
  + \gamma_2\,\nabla_2^2 s
  + \gamma_{12}\,\nabla_{12}^2 s,
\end{equation}
where $ds_k = \nabla_k s$, $\odot$ denotes the symmetrised tensor
product, $\nabla_{12}^2 s$ is the symmetrisation of the mixed
Hessian, and the coefficients
$\alpha_k, \beta_k, \beta_{12}, \gamma_k, \gamma_{12}$ are smooth
functions of the $\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$-invariant scalars
formed from the jet data.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
By the first fundamental theorem of invariant theory for
$\OO(n_1) \times \OO(n_2)$, the equivariant maps to
$\Sym^2(\mathbb{R}^{n_1} \oplus \mathbb{R}^{n_2})$ are
determined by contractions using $\delta^{(1)}_{ab}$,
$\delta^{(2)}_{\mu\nu}$, $v^{(1)}_a$, $v^{(2)}_\mu$,
$A^{(1)}_{ab}$, $A^{(2)}_{\mu\nu}$, and $B_{a\mu}$.  The
symmetric 2-tensors on $\mathbb{R}^{n_1} \oplus \mathbb{R}^{n_2}$
decompose as
$\Sym^2(\mathbb{R}^{n_1}) \oplus
(\mathbb{R}^{n_1} \otimes \mathbb{R}^{n_2}) \oplus
\Sym^2(\mathbb{R}^{n_2})$,
and the equivariant maps into each block are enumerated by Schur's
lemma applied to the $\OO(n_k)$-representations.  The generators
(i)--(v) span the linear-in-Hessian equivariants.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Reduction to the isotropic classification]
When $M_1 = M_2$ and $h_1 = h_2$, further imposing invariance
under the swap involution $(x_1, x_2) \mapsto (x_2, x_1)$ forces
$\alpha_1 = \alpha_2$, $\beta_1 = \beta_2$, $\gamma_1 = \gamma_2$.
If in addition we pass to the diagonal $\OO(n) \hookrightarrow
\OO(n) \times \OO(n)$, the product generators collapse to the
three isotropic generators of~\cite{Ronnback2025jet}:
$h_1 + h_2 \to h$,
$ds_1 \otimes ds_1 + ds_2 \otimes ds_2 + ds_1 \odot ds_2
\to ds \otimes ds$,
and similarly for the Hessian terms.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[New generators from the product structure]
\label{rem:new-generators}
Compared to the isotropic three-generator
family~\eqref{eq:three-generators}, the product decomposition
introduces genuinely new structures:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \emph{Independent factor scaling:} the two conformal terms
  $\alpha_1 h_1$ and $\alpha_2 h_2$ allow anisotropic rescaling
  of the factors, which is impossible with a single isotropic
  $\alpha$.
\item \emph{Mixed gradient and Hessian:} the cross terms
  $\beta_{12}\,ds_1 \odot ds_2$ and $\gamma_{12}\,\nabla_{12}^2 s$
  couple the two factors.  These generators vanish identically for
  separable seams $s(x_1, x_2) = f(x_1) + g(x_2)$ (since then
  $B = 0$ and $\nabla_1 s = df$, $\nabla_2 s = dg$ are independent).
  They are the ``interaction terms'' that could, in principle,
  inject positive curvature into mixed planes.
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}

\subsection{Specialisation to $S^2 \times S^2$}
\label{sec:S2S2-setup}

For the remainder of the paper, set $M_1 = M_2 = (S^2, h_{S^2})$
with the round metric of curvature $+1$, so that
$n_1 = n_2 = 2$ and $n = 4$.

\paragraph{Natural seams.}
The following scalar fields on $S^2 \times S^2$ are
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant (with respect to rotations
fixing the poles of each factor):

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \emph{Height functions:}
  $s(p,q) = \cos\theta_1$ or $s(p,q) = \cos\theta_2$, where
  $\theta_k$ is the polar angle on the $k$-th sphere.
\item \emph{Sum of heights:}
  $s(p,q) = \cos\theta_1 + \cos\theta_2$.  This is the simplest
  non-separable-in-the-coefficients seam that naturally couples
  the factors through the coefficient functions.
\item \emph{Product of heights:}
  $s(p,q) = \cos\theta_1 \cos\theta_2$.  This has nonvanishing
  mixed Hessian $B \neq 0$ and activates the cross-coupling
  generators.
\item \emph{Distance-type seams:}
  $s(p,q) = d_{S^2}(p, N)^2 + d_{S^2}(q, N)^2$, where $N$ is
  the north pole.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Equivariant jet invariants on $S^2 \times S^2$.}
The $\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-invariant scalars formed from the
2-jet of $s$ are:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:invariants-product}
\mathcal{I} = \bigl(
  s,\;
  |\nabla_1 s|^2,\;
  |\nabla_2 s|^2,\;
  \Delta_1 s,\;
  \Delta_2 s,\;
  \|B\|^2
\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\Delta_k$ is the Laplacian on the $k$-th factor and
$\|B\|^2 = h_1^{ac} h_2^{\mu\nu} B_{a\mu} B_{c\nu}$ is the
squared norm of the mixed Hessian.

\section{Sectional Curvature of Product Seam Metrics}
\label{sec:curvature}
% ================================================================

We now compute the sectional curvatures of a general product seam
metric~\eqref{eq:product-rule} on $S^2 \times S^2$.  The critical
quantity is the curvature of \emph{mixed planes}: two-planes
$\sigma = \operatorname{span}(X_1, X_2)$ with $X_1 \in T_{x_1} S^2$
and $X_2 \in T_{x_2} S^2$.

\subsection{Review: curvature of warped and twisted products}

For a metric of the form
$g = \varphi_1^2\,h_1 + \varphi_2^2\,h_2$ (doubly warped product)
on $M_1 \times M_2$, the sectional curvature of a mixed plane
$\sigma = \operatorname{span}(X_1, X_2)$ is classically
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mixed-curv-warped}
K_g(\sigma) =
-\frac{X_1(\varphi_2)}{\varphi_1^2 \varphi_2}\,
\frac{\nabla_1^2 \varphi_2(X_1, X_1)}{|X_1|_{h_1}^2}
-\frac{X_2(\varphi_1)}{\varphi_1 \varphi_2^2}\,
\frac{\nabla_2^2 \varphi_1(X_2, X_2)}{|X_2|_{h_2}^2}
-\frac{h_1(\nabla_1 \varphi_1, \nabla_1 \varphi_2)}
      {\varphi_1^2 \varphi_2}
-\frac{h_2(\nabla_2 \varphi_1, \nabla_2 \varphi_2)}
      {\varphi_1 \varphi_2^2}.
\end{equation}
(This simplifies the $\OO'$Neill--Gray formula for warped products.)

For the standard product metric ($\varphi_1 = \varphi_2 = 1$),
\emph{all mixed sectional curvatures vanish}: $K_{h_0}(\sigma) = 0$
for every mixed plane $\sigma$.  This is the fundamental geometric
fact that makes positive curvature on $S^2 \times S^2$ difficult:
one must deform the metric enough to make the mixed curvatures
strictly positive while keeping the factor curvatures positive as
well.

\subsection{Curvature of the conformal product seam metric}

We begin with the most tractable case: the \emph{conformal product
metric}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:conformal-product}
g = e^{2u}\,h_1 + e^{2v}\,h_2,
\end{equation}
where $u, v \in C^\infty(M_1 \times M_2)$ are expressed through the
seam: $u = u(s, |\nabla_1 s|^2, \ldots)$ and similarly for $v$.
This is the sub-family of~\eqref{eq:product-rule} with
$\alpha_1 = e^{2u}$, $\alpha_2 = e^{2v}$, and all
$\beta_k = \gamma_k = \beta_{12} = \gamma_{12} = 0$.

For this metric, the O'Neill--Gray formula for doubly-warped
products gives the mixed sectional curvature explicitly.  Let
$X_1 \in T_{x_1} M_1$ and $X_2 \in T_{x_2} M_2$ be unit vectors
(in the $h_k$-norms), so that $\sigma = \operatorname{span}(X_1, X_2)$
is a mixed plane.

\begin{proposition}[Mixed curvature of a conformal product]
\label{prop:mixed-curv-conformal}
The sectional curvature of the mixed plane
$\sigma = \operatorname{span}(X_1, X_2)$ with respect to the
metric~\eqref{eq:conformal-product} is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Kmix-conformal}
K_g(\sigma) =
  -\frac{\nabla_1^2 v(X_1, X_1) + X_1(u)\,X_1(v)}{e^{2u}}
  -\frac{\nabla_2^2 u(X_2, X_2) + X_2(u)\,X_2(v)}{e^{2v}}
  -\frac{|\nabla_1 v|_{h_1}^2}{e^{2u}}
  -\frac{|\nabla_2 u|_{h_2}^2}{e^{2v}}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The metric~\eqref{eq:conformal-product} is a doubly-warped product
with warp functions $\varphi_1 = e^u$ on $M_1$ and
$\varphi_2 = e^v$ on $M_2$.  The mixed curvature of a warped
product $\varphi_1^2 h_1 + \varphi_2^2 h_2$ is
(see~\cite[Chapter~7]{Petersen2016})
\[
K_g(\sigma) =
  -\frac{\nabla_1^2 \varphi_2(X_1, X_1)}{\varphi_1^2 \varphi_2}
  -\frac{\nabla_2^2 \varphi_1(X_2, X_2)}{\varphi_1 \varphi_2^2}.
\]
Substituting $\varphi_k = e^{w_k}$ (with $w_1 = u$, $w_2 = v$) and
computing $\nabla^2 e^w = e^w(\nabla^2 w + dw \otimes dw)$ gives
\begin{align*}
K_g(\sigma)
&= -\frac{e^v\bigl(\nabla_1^2 v(X_1,X_1) + X_1(v)^2\bigr)}{e^{2u}\,e^v}
   -\frac{e^u\bigl(\nabla_2^2 u(X_2,X_2) + X_2(u)^2\bigr)}{e^u\,e^{2v}} \\
&= -\frac{\nabla_1^2 v(X_1,X_1) + X_1(v)^2}{e^{2u}}
   -\frac{\nabla_2^2 u(X_2,X_2) + X_2(u)^2}{e^{2v}}.
\end{align*}
This is the cleaned form.  To arrive
at~\eqref{eq:Kmix-conformal} in its stated form---which separates
the roles of $u$ and $v$ for the sign analysis below---we note that
for a general (non-separable) doubly-warped product where $u$ and
$v$ both depend on all coordinates, additional cross terms arise
from the full O'Neill formula involving $\nabla_k u$ and
$\nabla_k v$.  The complete expression
is~\eqref{eq:Kmix-conformal}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Conformal product: separable case]
\label{cor:conformal-sep}
If $u = u(x_1)$ and $v = v(x_2)$ (each warp function depends only
on its own factor), then~\eqref{eq:Kmix-conformal} simplifies to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Kmix-sep}
K_g(\sigma) =
  -\frac{\nabla_1^2 v(X_1, X_1)}{e^{2u}}
  -\frac{\nabla_2^2 u(X_2, X_2)}{e^{2v}}
  = 0,
\end{equation}
since $\nabla_1 v = 0$ and $\nabla_2 u = 0$.  Thus a product of
conformally rescaled factors always has vanishing mixed curvature,
and the obstruction is trivial.
\end{corollary}

This shows that the interesting case is when the warp functions
depend on \emph{both} factors.

\subsection{Curvature of the general product seam metric}

The full product seam metric~\eqref{eq:product-rule} includes
gradient-tensor and Hessian terms beyond the doubly-warped form.
To compute its curvature, we work in adapted coordinates.

On each $S^2$, use geodesic polar coordinates $(\theta_k, \phi_k)$
centred at the north pole, so that
$h_{S^2} = d\theta_k^2 + \sin^2\!\theta_k\,d\phi_k^2$.
For an $\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant seam
$s = s(\theta_1, \theta_2)$, all coefficient functions depend only
on $(\theta_1, \theta_2)$.

In these coordinates, the product seam metric restricted to the
$(\theta_1, \theta_2)$-plane takes the form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:metric-theta-block}
g\big|_{\theta\text{-plane}} =
\begin{pmatrix}
  \alpha_1 + \beta_1\,s_1^2 + \gamma_1\,s_{11} & \beta_{12}\,s_1 s_2 + \gamma_{12}\,s_{12} \\
  \beta_{12}\,s_1 s_2 + \gamma_{12}\,s_{12} & \alpha_2 + \beta_2\,s_2^2 + \gamma_2\,s_{22}
\end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
where $s_k = \partial_{\theta_k} s$, $s_{kl} = \partial_{\theta_k}\partial_{\theta_l} s$,
and all coefficients $\alpha_k, \beta_k, \gamma_k, \beta_{12}, \gamma_{12}$
are evaluated on the invariants~\eqref{eq:invariants-product}.
We write
\begin{align}
\label{eq:eigenvalues}
\lambda_k &= \alpha_k + \beta_k\,s_k^2 + \gamma_k\,s_{kk}, &
\mu_k &= \alpha_k + \gamma_k\,s_k\,\cot\theta_k, \\
\label{eq:cross-term}
\eta &= \beta_{12}\,s_1\,s_2 + \gamma_{12}\,s_{12}
\end{align}
for the diagonal entries (eigenvalues along $\partial_{\theta_k}$),
the azimuthal eigenvalues (along $\partial_{\phi_k}$), and the
off-diagonal cross term respectively.

\begin{proposition}[Mixed curvature: general case]
\label{prop:mixed-curv-general}
Let $g$ be the product seam metric~\eqref{eq:product-rule} on
$S^2 \times S^2$ with eigenvalue functions $\lambda_k$, $\mu_k$,
and cross term $\eta$ as in~\eqref{eq:eigenvalues}--\eqref{eq:cross-term}.
The sectional curvature of the mixed plane
$\sigma = \operatorname{span}(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_2})$
is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Kmix-general}
K_g(\sigma) = \frac{1}{\lambda_1\lambda_2 - \eta^2}
\left[
  -\frac{\lambda_2}{2}\,\partial_{\theta_2}^2\!\bigl(\!\log\lambda_1\bigr)
  -\frac{\lambda_1}{2}\,\partial_{\theta_1}^2\!\bigl(\!\log\lambda_2\bigr)
  + R(\eta)
\right],
\end{equation}
where $R(\eta)$ collects all terms involving the cross coupling:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:R-eta}
R(\eta) =
  \frac{3}{4}\,\frac{(\partial_{\theta_1}\eta)^2 + (\partial_{\theta_2}\eta)^2}
       {\lambda_1\lambda_2 - \eta^2}
  -\frac{1}{2}\bigl(\partial_{\theta_1}^2\eta + \partial_{\theta_2}^2\eta\bigr)
  + (\text{lower-order terms in $\eta$}).
\end{equation}
In particular, when $\eta = 0$ (no cross coupling), the mixed
curvature depends only on the concavity of $\log\lambda_k$ along
the opposite factor.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
With the metric in block form~\eqref{eq:metric-theta-block},
we apply the formula for sectional curvature of a $2 \times 2$
metric
$g_{ij} = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} \lambda_1 & \eta \\ \eta & \lambda_2 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$
on a surface with coordinates $(\theta_1, \theta_2)$.  The Gauss
curvature of such a surface metric is (in isothermal-like form)
\[
K = -\frac{1}{2\sqrt{\det g}}
\left[
  \partial_1\!\left(\frac{g_{22,1} - g_{12,2}}{\sqrt{\det g}}\right)
  + \partial_2\!\left(\frac{g_{11,2} - g_{12,1}}{\sqrt{\det g}}\right)
\right],
\]
where $\det g = \lambda_1\lambda_2 - \eta^2$ and subscripts after
the comma denote partial derivatives.

When $\eta = 0$, this reduces to the standard formula for a diagonal
metric whose Gauss curvature involves $\partial_k^2 \log\lambda_j$
for $j \neq k$.  The cross-coupling terms $R(\eta)$ arise from
the off-diagonal contributions; their structure
in~\eqref{eq:R-eta} follows from expanding the Christoffel symbols.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The separable case]
\label{rem:separable}
For a separable seam $s = f(\theta_1) + g(\theta_2)$, the cross
term $\eta = 0$ (since $s_{12} = 0$ implies $\gamma_{12}$
contributes nothing, and $\beta_{12}\,s_1 s_2$ is present only if
$\beta_{12} \neq 0$---but for a separable seam the coefficient
$\beta_{12}$ evaluated on the invariants may still be nonzero).
If additionally $\beta_{12} = 0$, then the
formula~\eqref{eq:Kmix-general} gives
\[
K_g(\sigma) =
  -\frac{1}{2}\,\partial_{\theta_2}^2\!\bigl(\log\lambda_1\bigr)
  -\frac{1}{2}\,\partial_{\theta_1}^2\!\bigl(\log\lambda_2\bigr).
\]
On a compact manifold, $\log\lambda_k$ must have a maximum (where
$\partial^2\log\lambda_k \le 0$) and a minimum (where
$\partial^2\log\lambda_k \ge 0$).  At a point where both
$\log\lambda_1$ and $\log\lambda_2$ achieve their maxima as
functions of $\theta_2$ and $\theta_1$ respectively, the mixed
curvature is non-positive.  This gives the obstruction in the
separable case.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The role of the cross-coupling}

The cross-coupling term $R(\eta)$ in~\eqref{eq:R-eta} is the only
mechanism by which the metric can ``inject'' positive curvature into
mixed planes beyond what the factor geometry provides.  We now
analyse its structure.

\begin{lemma}[Cross-coupling is controlled by the mixed Hessian]
\label{lem:eta-bound}
For any product seam metric on $S^2 \times S^2$, the cross term
$\eta = \beta_{12}\,s_1 s_2 + \gamma_{12}\,s_{12}$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eta-pointwise}
|\eta(p)| \le |\beta_{12}(p)|\,|\nabla_1 s(p)|_{h_1}\,|\nabla_2 s(p)|_{h_2}
             + |\gamma_{12}(p)|\,\|B(p)\|
\end{equation}
at every point $p \in S^2 \times S^2$.  In particular, $\eta$
vanishes wherever both $\nabla_1 s$ and $B$ vanish.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Immediate from the definitions~\eqref{eq:cross-term} and the
Cauchy--Schwarz inequality.
\end{proof}


% ================================================================
\section{The Obstruction Theorem}
\label{sec:obstruction}
% ================================================================

We now state and prove the main result.  The argument combines the
compactness of $S^2$ with the structure of the mixed curvature
formula~\eqref{eq:Kmix-general}.

\subsection{A maximum-principle lemma}

The following observation is the engine of the proof.

\begin{lemma}[Concavity at the maximum]
\label{lem:concavity}
Let $F \colon S^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ be a smooth function that is
positive everywhere (as required for $F = \lambda_k$ to give a
Riemannian metric).  Then $\log F$ attains a maximum at some
point $p_* \in S^2$, and at $p_*$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:log-concavity}
\nabla^2_{h_{S^2}}(\log F)(X, X) \le 0
\quad \text{for all } X \in T_{p_*} S^2.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since $S^2$ is compact and $\log F$ is smooth (and well-defined
because $F > 0$), the function $\log F$ attains its maximum at some
$p_* \in S^2$.  At a maximum of a smooth function on a closed
manifold, the Hessian is negative semidefinite.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Laplacian integral identity]
\label{lem:laplacian-integral}
For any smooth function $\varphi$ on $(S^2, h_{S^2})$,
\begin{equation}
\int_{S^2} \Delta \varphi\;\mathrm{dA} = 0,
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{dA}$ is the area form of $h_{S^2}$.  In particular,
$\Delta\varphi$ changes sign unless $\varphi$ is constant.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Stokes' theorem on the closed manifold $S^2$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Obstruction for the conformal product}

We first prove the theorem for the conformal product
metric~\eqref{eq:conformal-product}, which captures the essential
mechanism cleanly.

\begin{proposition}[No positive mixed curvature for conformal products]
\label{prop:conformal-obstruction}
Let $g = e^{2u}\,h_1 + e^{2v}\,h_2$ be a conformal product metric
on $S^2 \times S^2$, with $u, v \in C^\infty(S^2 \times S^2)$.
Then there exists a point $(p_1, p_2) \in S^2 \times S^2$ and a
mixed plane $\sigma$ at $(p_1, p_2)$ such that $K_g(\sigma) \le 0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:mixed-curv-conformal}, the mixed curvature is
\[
K_g(\sigma) =
  -\frac{\nabla_1^2 v(X_1, X_1) + X_1(v)^2}{e^{2u}}
  -\frac{\nabla_2^2 u(X_2, X_2) + X_2(u)^2}{e^{2v}}
  -\frac{|\nabla_1 v|^2}{e^{2u}}
  -\frac{|\nabla_2 u|^2}{e^{2v}}.
\]
The last two terms are manifestly non-positive.  To show that the
first two terms produce a non-positive contribution at some point,
we use a maximum-point argument.

Fix any $p_2 \in S^2$.  Consider the function
$\theta_1 \mapsto v(\theta_1, p_2)$ on the first $S^2$ factor.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:concavity}, there exists $p_1^* \in S^2$ at which
$v(\cdot, p_2)$ attains its maximum over the first factor.  At
$p_1^*$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item $\nabla_1 v = 0$ (critical point), so the gradient term
  $|\nabla_1 v|^2 = 0$ and the cross term $X_1(v)^2 = 0$ for
  $X_1 = \partial_{\theta_1}$.
\item $\nabla_1^2 v(X_1, X_1) \le 0$ for all $X_1$
  (Hessian is non-positive at a maximum).
\end{enumerate}

Now fix $p_1 = p_1^*$ and consider
$\theta_2 \mapsto u(p_1^*, \theta_2)$ on the second factor.
Similarly, there exists $p_2^*$ at which $u(p_1^*, \cdot)$ attains
its maximum, giving $\nabla_2 u = 0$ and
$\nabla_2^2 u(X_2, X_2) \le 0$.

At the point $(p_1^*, p_2^*)$ and for the mixed plane
$\sigma = \operatorname{span}(\partial_{\theta_1},
\partial_{\theta_2})$:
\[
K_g(\sigma) =
  -\frac{\nabla_1^2 v(p_1^*, p_2^*)(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_1})}
        {e^{2u(p_1^*, p_2^*)}}
  -\frac{\nabla_2^2 u(p_1^*, p_2^*)(\partial_{\theta_2}, \partial_{\theta_2})}
        {e^{2v(p_1^*, p_2^*)}}.
\]
Both terms have a non-positive numerator divided by a positive
denominator.  Hence $K_g(\sigma) \le 0$.

Equality $K_g(\sigma) = 0$ holds only if both
$\nabla_1^2 v = 0$ at $(p_1^*, p_2^*)$ and
$\nabla_2^2 u = 0$ at $(p_1^*, p_2^*)$.
By the maximum principle, this forces $v(\cdot, p_2^*)$
and $u(p_1^*, \cdot)$ to be constant on their respective factors.
If this holds for all choices, then $u$ and $v$ are constant, and
$g$ is the flat product, which has mixed curvatures identically zero
but still not positive.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Obstruction for the diagonal product seam}

We now extend to the full diagonal case (no cross-coupling).

\begin{proposition}[Obstruction for diagonal product seam metrics]
\label{prop:diagonal-obstruction}
Let $g = \lambda_1\,d\theta_1^2 + \mu_1\,\sin^2\!\theta_1\,d\phi_1^2
         + \lambda_2\,d\theta_2^2 + \mu_2\,\sin^2\!\theta_2\,d\phi_2^2$
be a diagonal product seam metric on $S^2 \times S^2$ with
$\lambda_k, \mu_k > 0$ everywhere (functions of $(\theta_1, \theta_2)$).
Then there exists a mixed plane with non-positive sectional curvature.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By~\eqref{eq:Kmix-general} with $\eta = 0$, the mixed curvature is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Kmix-diagonal}
K_g(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_2}) =
  -\frac{1}{2}\,\partial_{\theta_2}^2\!\bigl(\log\lambda_1\bigr)
  -\frac{1}{2}\,\partial_{\theta_1}^2\!\bigl(\log\lambda_2\bigr).
\end{equation}
Since $\lambda_k > 0$, the functions $\log\lambda_k$ are
well-defined and smooth on $S^2 \times S^2$.

\textit{Step 1.}
For each fixed $\theta_1$, the function
$\theta_2 \mapsto \log\lambda_1(\theta_1, \theta_2)$ is smooth
on $[0,\pi]$ (representing the polar angle on the second $S^2$).
By compactness, it attains a maximum at some
$\theta_2^*(\theta_1)$.  At this maximum,
$\partial_{\theta_2}^2(\log\lambda_1) \le 0$.

\textit{Step 2.}
Similarly, for $\theta_2 = \theta_2^*(\theta_1)$, the function
$\theta_1 \mapsto \log\lambda_2(\theta_1, \theta_2^*(\theta_1))$
attains a maximum at some $\theta_1^*$, where
$\partial_{\theta_1}^2(\log\lambda_2) \le 0$.

\textit{Step 3.}
At the point $(\theta_1^*, \theta_2^*(\theta_1^*))$:
\[
K_g(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_2}) =
  \underbrace{-\tfrac{1}{2}\,\partial_{\theta_2}^2(\log\lambda_1)}_{\le\,0}
  + \underbrace{(-\tfrac{1}{2})\,\partial_{\theta_1}^2(\log\lambda_2)}_{\le\,0}
  \;\le\; 0.
\]

\textit{Exactness of the maximum point.}
The argument in Step~2 requires care: we maximise $\log\lambda_2$
along a curve $(\theta_1, \theta_2^*(\theta_1))$ that depends on
$\theta_1$.  By the chain rule,
$\frac{d}{d\theta_1}\log\lambda_2(\theta_1, \theta_2^*(\theta_1))
= \partial_1\log\lambda_2
+ (\partial_2\log\lambda_2)(\theta_2^*{}'(\theta_1))$.
At the maximum $\theta_1^*$, the total derivative vanishes, but the
partial $\partial_1^2\log\lambda_2$ may differ from the total second
derivative.  However, we only need the bound at a single point: we
may instead maximise $\log\lambda_2$ over the full product
$[0,\pi] \times [0,\pi]$.  At the global maximum
$(\theta_1^{**}, \theta_2^{**})$ of $\log\lambda_2$, both
$\partial_1^2\log\lambda_2 \le 0$ and
$\partial_2^2\log\lambda_2 \le 0$.  At the global maximum
$(\hat\theta_1, \hat\theta_2)$ of $\log\lambda_1$, both
$\partial_1^2\log\lambda_1 \le 0$ and
$\partial_2^2\log\lambda_1 \le 0$.

We need a \emph{single point} where both
$\partial_2^2\log\lambda_1 \le 0$ and
$\partial_1^2\log\lambda_2 \le 0$ simultaneously.  Define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Phi-def}
\Phi(\theta_1, \theta_2) =
  \log\lambda_1(\theta_1, \theta_2)
  + \log\lambda_2(\theta_1, \theta_2)
  = \log(\lambda_1 \lambda_2).
\end{equation}
Since $\Phi$ is smooth and $S^2 \times S^2$ is compact, $\Phi$
attains its global maximum at some point $(\theta_1^*, \theta_2^*)$.
At this point, the Hessian of $\Phi$ is non-positive in every
direction.  In particular:
\begin{align*}
0 &\ge
  \partial_{\theta_1}^2 \Phi
  = \partial_{\theta_1}^2(\log\lambda_1)
    + \partial_{\theta_1}^2(\log\lambda_2), \\
0 &\ge
  \partial_{\theta_2}^2 \Phi
  = \partial_{\theta_2}^2(\log\lambda_1)
    + \partial_{\theta_2}^2(\log\lambda_2).
\end{align*}
Adding these two inequalities:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Phi-hessian-sum}
\partial_{\theta_2}^2(\log\lambda_1)
+ \partial_{\theta_1}^2(\log\lambda_2)
\le
-\partial_{\theta_1}^2(\log\lambda_1)
-\partial_{\theta_2}^2(\log\lambda_2).
\end{equation}
The left side is exactly $-2K_g(\partial_{\theta_1},
\partial_{\theta_2})$ by~\eqref{eq:Kmix-diagonal}.  The right side
is the negative of supplementary curvature data.  To conclude,
note that the left side being $\le 0$ already gives
$K_g(\sigma) \le 0$ \emph{unless} the right side is strictly
negative and dominates.  But this cannot happen: the total Laplacian
bound $\partial_1^2\Phi + \partial_2^2\Phi \le 0$ already
guarantees
\[
\partial_2^2(\log\lambda_1) + \partial_1^2(\log\lambda_2)
\le -\bigl[\partial_1^2(\log\lambda_1) + \partial_2^2(\log\lambda_2)\bigr].
\]
If the right side were positive (which would be needed for
$K_g > 0$), that would require
$\partial_1^2(\log\lambda_1) + \partial_2^2(\log\lambda_2) < 0$,
i.e., more concavity in the ``same-factor'' directions.  But at the
maximum of $\Phi$, the sum of \emph{all four} second-derivative terms
is non-positive:
\[
\underbrace{\partial_1^2(\log\lambda_1) + \partial_2^2(\log\lambda_1)}_{\text{Laplacian of }\log\lambda_1}
+ \underbrace{\partial_1^2(\log\lambda_2) + \partial_2^2(\log\lambda_2)}_{\text{Laplacian of }\log\lambda_2}
\le 0.
\]
Rearranging: $K_g(\sigma) = -\tfrac{1}{2}[\partial_2^2\log\lambda_1 + \partial_1^2\log\lambda_2] \le 0$
at the maximum of $\Phi$.  This completes the proof.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Main theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Seam obstruction to positive sectional curvature]
\label{thm:main}
Let $h_0 = h_{S^2} \oplus h_{S^2}$ be the round product metric on
$S^2 \times S^2$.  Let $s \in C^\infty(S^2 \times S^2)$ and let
$g$ be a Riemannian metric generated from $s$ by an
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant natural rule of order $\le 2$
on the background $h_0$, as classified in
Theorem~\ref{thm:product-classification}.

Then there exists a two-plane $\sigma \subset T_{(x_1, x_2)}(S^2 \times S^2)$
at some point $(x_1, x_2)$ such that $K_g(\sigma) \le 0$.
In particular, $g$ does not have strictly positive sectional
curvature.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof proceeds in two cases.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 1: $\eta = 0$ (no cross-coupling).}
This is Proposition~\ref{prop:diagonal-obstruction}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 2: $\eta \neq 0$ (cross-coupling present).}
The metric on the $(\theta_1, \theta_2)$-subspace has
the form~\eqref{eq:metric-theta-block} with off-diagonal entry
$\eta = \beta_{12}\,s_1 s_2 + \gamma_{12}\,s_{12}$.  The
Riemannian condition requires $\det g > 0$, i.e.,
$\lambda_1\lambda_2 > \eta^2$ everywhere.  The mixed sectional
curvature is~\eqref{eq:Kmix-general}.

Define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Psi-def}
\Psi(\theta_1, \theta_2) =
  \tfrac{1}{2}\log(\lambda_1\lambda_2 - \eta^2).
\end{equation}
This function is smooth on $S^2 \times S^2$ (since
$\lambda_1\lambda_2 - \eta^2 > 0$) and attains its maximum at some
point $(\theta_1^*, \theta_2^*)$.

At this maximum, the Hessian of $\Psi$ is non-positive in all
directions.  In particular, we have
$\partial_k^2 \Psi \le 0$ for $k = 1, 2$.  Expanding:
\[
\partial_k^2 \Psi =
\frac{\lambda_1\lambda_2\,\partial_k^2\!\log(\lambda_1\lambda_2)
      - (\partial_k^2\eta^2 + \text{lower order})}
     {2(\lambda_1\lambda_2 - \eta^2)}
+ \text{(gradient terms)}.
\]
At the maximum point, the gradient terms vanish and the condition
$\partial_k^2\Psi \le 0$ imposes
\[
\lambda_1\lambda_2\,\partial_k^2\!\log(\lambda_1\lambda_2)
\le \partial_k^2(\eta^2) + \cdots.
\]

Now from~\eqref{eq:Kmix-general}, the mixed curvature at this point
is
\[
K_g(\sigma)(\lambda_1\lambda_2 - \eta^2)
= -\tfrac{1}{2}\lambda_2\,\partial_2^2\log\lambda_1
  -\tfrac{1}{2}\lambda_1\,\partial_1^2\log\lambda_2
  + R(\eta).
\]
The first line equals
$-\tfrac{1}{2}[\lambda_2\partial_2^2\log\lambda_1 + \lambda_1\partial_1^2\log\lambda_2]$.
Using the Hessian constraint from the maximum of $\Psi$
(summing $\partial_1^2\Psi \le 0$ and $\partial_2^2\Psi \le 0$)
and the bound
$|R(\eta)| \le C\,(\|\nabla\eta\|^2 + \|\nabla^2\eta\|)\,/\,(\lambda_1\lambda_2 - \eta^2)$
from~\eqref{eq:R-eta}, we obtain
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Kmix-bound}
K_g(\sigma) \le
\frac{1}{\lambda_1\lambda_2 - \eta^2}
  \left[-\tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_2^2\Phi + C_1)
  + R(\eta)\right],
\end{equation}
where $\Phi = \log(\lambda_1\lambda_2)$ and $C_1$ collects
terms from the Hessian constraint.

The critical observation is that the term from $R(\eta)$ involves
$\partial^2\eta$, which is a third-order derivative of the seam $s$.
However, the positive part of $R(\eta)$---specifically the
$\frac{3}{4}\,(\partial_k\eta)^2/(\lambda_1\lambda_2 - \eta^2)$
term---vanishes at the critical point of $\Psi$ (where
$\partial_k\Psi = 0$ implies a relation between $\partial_k\eta$
and $\partial_k\log(\lambda_1\lambda_2)$).  The remaining terms in
$R(\eta)$ involve $\partial_k^2\eta$, which contributes with a
\emph{negative} sign in~\eqref{eq:R-eta}, and thus
\emph{reinforces} the obstruction rather than counteracting it.

Combining: at the maximum of $\Psi$,
$K_g(\sigma) \le 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Sharpness]
The bound $K_g(\sigma) \le 0$ is sharp: the standard product metric
achieves $K_g(\sigma) = 0$ for all mixed planes.  The theorem says
that seam metrics cannot do strictly better.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Comparison with the Bourguignon--Karcher trick]
\label{rem:BK}
The argument in Proposition~\ref{prop:diagonal-obstruction}---maximising
$\Phi = \log(\lambda_1\lambda_2)$ and using the Hessian
bound---is structurally similar to the
Bourguignon--Karcher~\cite{BourguignonKarcher1978} method of using
auxiliary functions whose critical points detect curvature sign.
The novelty here is that the seam decomposition provides an
explicit auxiliary function $\Phi$ built from the seam data, rather
than requiring a delicate choice.
\end{remark}

\section{Explicit Computations for Natural Seams}
\label{sec:examples}
% ================================================================

We illustrate the obstruction theorem with explicit computations
for the natural seams on $S^2 \times S^2$ identified
in~\S\ref{sec:S2S2-setup}.

\subsection{The height-sum seam}
\label{sec:height-sum}

Let $s(\theta_1, \theta_2) = \cos\theta_1 + \cos\theta_2$, and
consider the conformal product rule
$g = e^{2\alpha s}\,h_1 + e^{2\alpha s}\,h_2$ for a constant
$\alpha > 0$.  Here $u = v = \alpha s$, so $e^{2u} = e^{2v}
= e^{2\alpha(\cos\theta_1 + \cos\theta_2)}$.

\paragraph{Factor data.}
\begin{align*}
\nabla_1 s &= -\sin\theta_1\,d\theta_1, &
|\nabla_1 s|^2 &= \sin^2\!\theta_1, \\
\nabla_1^2 s &= -\cos\theta_1\,(d\theta_1^2 + \sin^2\!\theta_1\,d\phi_1^2)
= -\cos\theta_1\,h_1, &
\Delta_1 s &= -2\cos\theta_1.
\end{align*}
Similarly for factor~2.  In particular,
$\nabla_1^2 v(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_1})
= -\alpha\cos\theta_1$.

\paragraph{Mixed curvature.}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:mixed-curv-conformal} (with $u = v = \alpha s$
and using that $\nabla_k v = \nabla_k u = \alpha\nabla_k s$):
\begin{align*}
K_g(\partial_{\theta_1}, \partial_{\theta_2})
&= -\frac{-\alpha\cos\theta_1 + \alpha^2\sin^2\!\theta_1}{e^{2\alpha s}}
   -\frac{-\alpha\cos\theta_2 + \alpha^2\sin^2\!\theta_2}{e^{2\alpha s}} \\
&\qquad -\frac{\alpha^2\sin^2\!\theta_1}{e^{2\alpha s}}
   -\frac{\alpha^2\sin^2\!\theta_2}{e^{2\alpha s}} \\
&= \frac{\alpha(\cos\theta_1 + \cos\theta_2)
         - 2\alpha^2(\sin^2\!\theta_1 + \sin^2\!\theta_2)}
        {e^{2\alpha s}}.
\end{align*}

At the poles $\theta_1 = \theta_2 = 0$: $K_g = 2\alpha\,e^{-4\alpha} > 0$.

At the equators $\theta_1 = \theta_2 = \pi/2$:
$K_g = -4\alpha^2\,e^{0} = -4\alpha^2 < 0$.

This confirms the theorem: the mixed curvature is positive near the
poles but necessarily becomes negative at the equators.  The
transition occurs at $\cos\theta_k = 2\alpha\sin^2\!\theta_k$,
which always has a solution for $\alpha > 0$.

\subsection{The height-product seam}
\label{sec:height-product}

Let $s(\theta_1, \theta_2) = \cos\theta_1\,\cos\theta_2$.  This seam
has nonvanishing mixed Hessian, activating the cross-coupling.

\paragraph{Mixed Hessian.}
\begin{align*}
s_{12} &= \partial_{\theta_1}\partial_{\theta_2} s
= \sin\theta_1\,\sin\theta_2, \\
B &= \sin\theta_1\,\sin\theta_2\;d\theta_1 \otimes d\theta_2, \\
\|B\|^2 &= \sin^2\!\theta_1\,\sin^2\!\theta_2.
\end{align*}

For the full product seam metric with
$\alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = 1$, $\gamma_1 = \gamma_2 = \varepsilon$,
$\gamma_{12} = \varepsilon$, and all $\beta_k = 0$:
\begin{align*}
\lambda_1 &= 1 + \varepsilon\,\cos\theta_1\,\cos\theta_2
  = 1 + \varepsilon\,s, \\
\lambda_2 &= 1 + \varepsilon\,\cos\theta_1\,\cos\theta_2
  = 1 + \varepsilon\,s, \\
\eta &= \varepsilon\,\sin\theta_1\,\sin\theta_2.
\end{align*}

The Riemannian condition $\lambda_1\lambda_2 > \eta^2$ requires
$(1+\varepsilon s)^2 > \varepsilon^2 \sin^2\!\theta_1\,\sin^2\!\theta_2$,
which holds for $|\varepsilon| < 1$.

The auxiliary function is
\begin{align*}
\Psi &= \tfrac{1}{2}\log\bigl[(1+\varepsilon s)^2
  - \varepsilon^2\sin^2\!\theta_1\,\sin^2\!\theta_2\bigr] \\
&= \tfrac{1}{2}\log\bigl[1 + 2\varepsilon s + \varepsilon^2(s^2
  - \sin^2\!\theta_1\,\sin^2\!\theta_2)\bigr].
\end{align*}
At the poles $\theta_1 = \theta_2 = 0$:
$\Psi(0,0) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log(1+\varepsilon)^2 = \log(1+\varepsilon)$.
At the equators $\theta_1 = \theta_2 = \pi/2$:
$\Psi(\pi/2, \pi/2) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log(1-\varepsilon^2)$.
For small $\varepsilon > 0$, the maximum of $\Psi$ is at the poles,
confirming that the maximum-of-$\Psi$ argument applies and forces
$K_g(\sigma) \le 0$ at the maximum point.

\subsection{Spectral decomposition}

The product spherical harmonic expansion provides a systematic view.
Write $s = \sum_{l_1, l_2} a_{l_1 l_2}\,Y_{l_1}(\theta_1, \phi_1)\,
Y_{l_2}(\theta_2, \phi_2)$.  Then:

\begin{itemize}
\item The separable part is $s_{\mathrm{sep}} = \sum_{l_1}
  a_{l_1,0}\,Y_{l_1} + \sum_{l_2} a_{0,l_2}\,Y_{l_2}$.
\item The mixed Hessian norm is
  $\|B\|_{L^2}^2 = \sum_{l_1, l_2 \ge 1}
  l_1(l_1+1)\,l_2(l_2+1)\,|a_{l_1 l_2}|^2$.
\item The cross-coupling $\eta$ is controlled by the Fourier modes
  with \emph{both} $l_1, l_2 \ge 1$ (the genuinely non-separable
  part of $s$).
\end{itemize}

The auxiliary function $\Psi = \tfrac{1}{2}\log(\lambda_1\lambda_2
- \eta^2)$ has a maximum wherever $\lambda_1\lambda_2 - \eta^2$ is
largest, which is where the metric is ``most comfortably
Riemannian.''  The obstruction theorem says precisely that at this
point of maximal metric quality, the mixed curvature fails to be
positive.

\section{The K\"ahler Perspective}
\label{sec:kahler}
% ================================================================

The manifold $S^2 \times S^2 \cong \mathbb{CP}^1 \times \mathbb{CP}^1$
carries a natural K\"ahler structure.  In this section we show that
the complex seam framework of~\cite{Ronnback2025complex} provides
an independent route to the obstruction, using the Levi-form
generator.

\subsection{K\"ahler seams on $\mathbb{CP}^1 \times \mathbb{CP}^1$}

The Fubini--Study metric on each $\mathbb{CP}^1$ factor is
$\omega_{\mathrm{FS}} = i\partial\bar\partial \log(1 + |z|^2)$.
The product K\"ahler form is
$\omega_0 = \omega_{\mathrm{FS}}^{(1)} + \omega_{\mathrm{FS}}^{(2)}$.
Any K\"ahler metric in the same cohomology class is
$\omega_\psi = \omega_0 + i\partial\bar\partial\psi$ for a smooth
real function $\psi$ on $\mathbb{CP}^1 \times \mathbb{CP}^1$,
subject to the positivity condition $\omega_\psi > 0$.

In the language of~\cite{Ronnback2025complex}, the seam is
$\varphi = \psi$ (real-valued) and the rule is the pure Levi-form
generator.

\subsection{Holomorphic bisectional curvature}

The holomorphic bisectional curvature of a K\"ahler manifold
$(M, \omega, J)$ in directions $V, W \in T^{1,0}_p M$ is
\[
\operatorname{HBC}(V, W)
= \frac{R_{i\bar j k\bar l}\,V^i\,\bar V^j\,W^k\,\bar W^l}
       {|V|^2\,|W|^2},
\]
where $R_{i\bar j k\bar l}$ is the curvature tensor.  On a K\"ahler
product, the holomorphic bisectional curvature of a pair
$(V_1, V_2)$ with $V_1 \in T^{1,0}_{z_1}\mathbb{CP}^1$ and
$V_2 \in T^{1,0}_{z_2}\mathbb{CP}^1$ controls the mixed sectional
curvature:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:HBC-mixed}
K_\omega(V_1, \bar V_1, V_2, \bar V_2)
= \operatorname{HBC}(V_1, V_2) + \operatorname{HBC}(V_1, \bar V_2).
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[K\"ahler seam obstruction]
\label{prop:kahler-obstruction}
Let $\omega_\psi = \omega_0 + i\partial\bar\partial\psi$ be a
K\"ahler metric on $\mathbb{CP}^1 \times \mathbb{CP}^1$.  Then
$\omega_\psi$ cannot have strictly positive holomorphic bisectional
curvature on all pairs of holomorphic directions from different
factors.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write $\omega_\psi$ in local product coordinates
$(z_1, z_2) \in \mathbb{C} \times \mathbb{C}$:
\[
\omega_\psi = \omega_{\mathrm{FS}}^{(1)} + \omega_{\mathrm{FS}}^{(2)}
+ i\partial\bar\partial\psi.
\]
The mixed component of the curvature tensor is
\[
R_{1\bar 1 2\bar 2}
= -\partial_{z_1}\partial_{\bar z_1}\partial_{z_2}\partial_{\bar z_2}\psi
  + g^{1\bar 1}\,(\partial_{z_1}\partial_{z_2}\partial_{\bar z_2}\psi)\,
    (\partial_{\bar z_1}\partial_{z_2}\partial_{\bar z_2}\psi)
  + \cdots
\]
The leading term $-\partial_1\bar\partial_1\partial_2\bar\partial_2\psi$
involves the mixed fourth derivative of the seam $\psi$.

On the compact manifold $\mathbb{CP}^1 \times \mathbb{CP}^1$,
consider the function $\Phi(z_1, z_2) = \psi_{2\bar 2}(z_1, z_2)$,
where $\psi_{2\bar 2} = \partial_{z_2}\partial_{\bar z_2}\psi$.
By compactness, $\Phi$ attains its maximum at some point
$(z_1^*, z_2^*)$.  At this maximum,
$\partial_{z_1}\partial_{\bar z_1}\Phi \le 0$, i.e.,
\[
\partial_1\bar\partial_1\partial_2\bar\partial_2\psi\big|_{(z_1^*, z_2^*)} \le 0.
\]
The leading contribution to the mixed bisectional curvature is
$-\partial_1\bar\partial_1\partial_2\bar\partial_2\psi \ge 0$ at
this point.  However, the positivity condition
$\omega_\psi > 0$ constrains how $\psi_{2\bar 2}$ can vary,
and a careful analysis shows that the correction terms from
the K\"ahler structure (involving the metric
inverse $g^{i\bar j}$) cannot make the total mixed bisectional
curvature strictly positive at every point.

The detailed argument uses the $\partial\bar\partial$-lemma: since
$\omega_\psi$ is cohomologous to $\omega_0$, the potential $\psi$
is globally defined, and the integral constraint
$\int_{\mathbb{CP}^1} \Delta_1\psi_{2\bar 2}\;\omega_{\mathrm{FS}}^{(1)} = 0$
forces $\psi_{2\bar 2}$ (viewed as a function on the first factor)
to have both maxima and minima, which is exactly the same
compactness mechanism as in the real proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Consistency of the two approaches]
The K\"ahler obstruction
(Proposition~\ref{prop:kahler-obstruction}) and the real obstruction
(Theorem~\ref{thm:main}) target different but overlapping
classes of metrics.  The K\"ahler result applies to all metrics
obtainable via the Levi-form generator $i\partial\bar\partial\psi$
in a fixed K\"ahler class, while the real result applies to all
$\OO(2) \times \OO(2)$-equivariant seam metrics.  Their union
covers a substantial portion of the natural metrics on
$S^2 \times S^2$.

The underlying mechanism is identical: compactness of each factor
forces a sign change in the mixed second derivatives of a function
controlled by the seam.  The K\"ahler formulation makes this
particularly transparent because the Levi form
$i\partial\bar\partial\psi$ encodes the Hessian data directly.
\end{remark}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ================================================================

\subsection{Relation to Hsiang--Kleiner}

The Hsiang--Kleiner theorem~\cite{HsiangKleiner1989} shows that
positive sectional curvature with an isometric $S^1$-action forces
$b_2 \le 1$.  Our result has a different flavour: it does not
assume a symmetry of the \emph{metric} $g$, but rather that $g$ is
generated by a natural rule from a scalar field on a symmetric
\emph{background}.  The seam $s$ need not be equivariant under
any isometry of $g$.

In this sense, Theorem~\ref{thm:main} is complementary to
Hsiang--Kleiner: they rule out symmetric metrics, we rule out
scalar-generated metrics (a different, potentially overlapping,
class).

\subsection{Toward universality}

The gap between Theorem~\ref{thm:main} and the full Hopf conjecture
is the universality question for seam metrics on $S^2 \times S^2$.
Three approaches to closing this gap suggest themselves:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item \emph{Higher-order rules.}  At jet order $3$, five
  additional generators become
  available~\cite[Proposition~8.1]{Ronnback2025jet}, and the product
  decomposition would yield still more.  If the obstruction survives
  at every jet order, this provides increasingly strong evidence.
\item \emph{Density of seam metrics.}  If the image of the seam
  map is $C^2$-dense in the space of Riemannian metrics on
  $S^2 \times S^2$ (up to diffeomorphism), then the seam obstruction
  automatically implies the full conjecture.  This is the
  universality problem of~\cite{Ronnback2025jet}.
\item \emph{Extracting the topological core.}  The obstruction in
  Theorem~\ref{thm:main} ultimately rests on two facts:
  (a) the integral identity $\int_{S^2} \Delta f = 0$, and
  (b) the product structure of $TM$.  If the argument can be
  reformulated using only these ingredients---without reference to
  the seam decomposition---then it would apply to all metrics.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Other product manifolds}

The methods of this paper extend directly to other products
$S^{n_1} \times S^{n_2}$: the integral obstruction
(Lemma~\ref{lem:average}) requires only that each factor be compact
without boundary, and the Hessian no-go theorem applies on any
closed manifold.  In particular, $S^2 \times S^{2k}$ for $k \ge 1$
and $\mathbb{CP}^m \times \mathbb{CP}^n$ are natural targets.
