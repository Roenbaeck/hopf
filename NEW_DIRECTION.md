Differential geometry is currently experiencing a "golden age" following the resolution of major 20th-century milestones like the Poincaré conjecture. While several "obdurate" problems such as the [Willmore conjecture](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11716) and Lawson conjecture have been solved in the last decade, many fundamental questions regarding curvature, geodesics, and the relationship between shape and spectrum remain open. [1, 2, 3, 4] 
1. Curvature and Topology Conjectures
These problems focus on how local constraints on curvature (like being "positive") dictate the overall global shape of a manifold. [5, 6] 

* Hopf Conjectures:
* Does $S^2 \times S^2$ admit a Riemannian metric with strictly positive sectional curvature?
   * Does every even-dimensional manifold with positive sectional curvature have a positive Euler characteristic?
* Bott's Conjecture: Asserts that every simply connected, nonnegatively curved manifold is rationally elliptic, meaning its rational homotopy groups have finite total rank.
* Positive Biorthogonal Curvature: A central open question is whether $S^2 \times T^2$ admits a metric with strictly positive biorthogonal curvature, a problem that has remained unsolved despite recent attempts using affine connections. [7, 8, 9, 10, 11] 

2. Geodesics and Minimal Surfaces
These problems investigate the existence and behavior of "shortest paths" and "optimal surfaces" within a space. [12, 13] 

* Closed Geodesics on the 3-Sphere: Does every Riemannian metric on the 3-sphere ($S^3$) possess infinitely many prime closed geodesics?
* The Carathéodory Conjecture: Concerns the number of umbilic points on a closed, convex surface. While some recent papers claim proofs involving [holomorphic discs](https://muse.jhu.edu/pub/423/article/968272/pdf) and symmetry-breaking, it remains a focal point of intense scrutiny in the field.
* Isoperimetric Problem in $\mathbb{CP}^2$: Identifying the exact surfaces that minimize area for a given volume in the complex projective plane remains unsolved. [1, 2, 7, 14, 15] 

3. Spectral Geometry: "Hearing the Shape" [16] 
Spectral geometry asks what information can be recovered from the spectrum of the Laplacian (the "frequencies" at which a manifold vibrates). [17] 

* Orbifold Singularities: Can one "hear" the presence of an orbifold singularity? This asks if there exists a pair of isospectral spaces where one is a smooth manifold and the other has singular points.
* Audible Properties: Determining which specific geometric properties (like volume, dimension, or specific curvature integrals) are fully determined by the spectrum remains a broad area of active research. [18, 19, 20, 21] 

4. Geometric Analysis and Flows
These problems involve partial differential equations (PDEs) that evolve a shape over time, such as Ricci flow. [22, 23, 24] 

* Einstein Metrics: Does every smooth manifold admit an [Einstein metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold) (where Ricci curvature is proportional to the metric)? For many manifolds, even the existence of such a metric is unknown.
* Yang-Mills Existence and Mass Gap: One of the Millennium Prize Problems, this problem uses differential geometry to describe the behavior of subatomic particles and remains one of the most difficult open questions in mathematical physics. [1, 25, 26, 27, 28]
