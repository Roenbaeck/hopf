$$ \int_Q K_{\text{orb}} \, dA + \int_{\partial Q} k_g \, ds + \sum \theta_i = 2\pi \chi(Q) = 2\pi $$

### 2. Attacking the Non-Equivariant Case (Extrema of the Hessian)
To extend your theorem to non-equivariant seams (where Hsiang-Kleiner cannot apply), you need a purely local analytic argument. 
Your Section 6 (the Kähler perspective) contains a brilliant insight that you can directly translate to the real, non-equivariant case. 

In the conformal case (Prop 4.3), you evaluated the curvature at the maximum of the metric conformal factors $u$ and $v$ to control $\nabla^2 u$ and $\nabla^2 v$. 
However, for a general non-equivariant seam, the metric $g$ contains $\nabla^2 s$. Therefore, the mixed curvature will contain **fourth derivatives** of $s$. To control fourth derivatives, you must look at the extrema of the *second* derivatives, not the extrema of $s$ itself.

**The actionable step:** 
1. Look at the general mixed curvature formula for $g = h_0 + \gamma_1 \nabla_1^2 s + \gamma_2 \nabla_2^2 s + \dots$
2. The leading term of the mixed curvature $K(X_1, X_2)$ will be proportional to $-\nabla_1^2 \nabla_2^2 s(X_1, X_1, X_2, X_2)$.
3. Fix a point $p_2 \in M_2$ and a vector $X_2$. Define the scalar function on the first factor: $\Phi(p_1) = \nabla_2^2 s|_{(p_1, p_2)}(X_2, X_2)$.
4. Because $M_1 = S^2$ is compact, Lemma 4.2 dictates that $\Phi$ must have a **minimum** at some point $p_1^*$.
5. At this minimum, the Laplacian/Hessian of $\Phi$ is positive semi-definite: $\nabla_1^2 \Phi(X_1, X_1) \ge 0$.
6. This implies that at $(p_1^*, p_2)$, the 4th derivative term $-\nabla_1^2 \nabla_2^2 s(X_1, X_1, X_2, X_2) \le 0$.

If you write out the full curvature tensor at this specific point, you will likely find that the leading order term is strictly non-positive, exactly mirroring your Kähler proof. This would crack the non-equivariant case wide open.

### 3. Formalizing "Universality" (Section 7.2)
To make your final discussion section hit harder, it would be beneficial to explicitly quantify the "gap" between seam metrics and the space of all Riemannian metrics using a degree-of-freedom argument.
*   A generic Riemannian metric on a 4-manifold depends locally on **10 independent functions** of 4 variables.
*   A seam metric $g_s$ depends on only **1 function** of 4 variables (the seam $s$), plus the choices of the rule coefficients $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$.
*   Therefore, the space of seam metrics is "small" (infinite codimension).

You can bridge this gap by bringing up the **Nash Embedding Theorem**. A generic metric can be globally generated by pulling back the Euclidean metric via an embedding into $\mathbb{R}^N$. This is equivalent to saying that *every* metric is a "multi-seam metric" generated by $N$ scalar functions $s_1, \dots, s_N$ (the embedding coordinates) via the rule $g = \sum ds_k \otimes ds_k$. 
By framing the ultimate goal as proving that the mixed-curvature obstruction persists for $N$-seam rules, you provide a clear, rigorous roadmap for how this specific framework could eventually prove the full Hopf conjecture.
